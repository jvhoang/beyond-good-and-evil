FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 14

Identifier: 14 • Natural Philosophy as Exposition • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 14 of Beyond Good and Evil returns to the theme of the senses and interpretation with a contrast between two fundamentally different modes of engaging the world: the plebeian sensualism of modern natural philosophy and the aristocratic resistance to the senses characteristic of Platonic thought.

"It is perhaps just dawning on five or six minds that natural philosophy is only a world-exposition and world-arrangement (according to us, if I may say so!) and NOT a world-explanation." Yet because it rests on "belief in the senses," it is long taken for more—for an actual explanation. It possesses "eyes and fingers of its own, ocular evidence and palpableness of its own," which "operates fascinatingly, persuasively, and CONVINCINGLY upon an age with fundamentally plebeian tastes." It follows "the canon of truth of eternal popular sensualism": only what can be "seen and felt" counts as clear or explained.

In contrast, "the charm of the Platonic mode of thought, which was an ARISTOCRATIC mode, consisted precisely in RESISTANCE to obvious sense-evidence." This resistance was practiced by people "who enjoyed even stronger and more fastidious senses than our contemporaries, but who knew how to find a higher triumph in remaining masters of them" through "pale, cold, grey conceptional networks which they threw over the motley whirl of the senses—the mob of the senses, as Plato said."

The enjoyment of this Platonic overcoming and interpreting of the world was of a different order from the pleasure offered by today's physicists, Darwinists, and anti-teleologists with their "principle of the 'smallest possible effort'" and "greatest possible blunder." The imperative "Where there is nothing more to see or to grasp, there is also nothing more for men to do" may be appropriate for "a hardy, laborious race of machinists and bridge-builders of the future," but it is not the only or highest imperative.

Key quotes (Kaufmann translation):
"It is perhaps just dawning on five or six minds that natural philosophy is only a world-exposition and world-arrangement (according to us, if I may say so!) and NOT a world-explanation."
"What is clear, what is 'explained'? Only that which can be seen and felt—one must pursue every problem thus far."
"the charm of the Platonic mode of thought, which was an ARISTOCRATIC mode, consisted precisely in RESISTANCE to obvious sense-evidence—perhaps among men who enjoyed even stronger and more fastidious senses than our contemporaries..."
"'Where there is nothing more to see or to grasp, there is also nothing more for men to do'—that is certainly an imperative different from the Platonic one, but it may notwithstanding be the right imperative for a hardy, laborious race of machinists and bridge-builders of the future..."

Philosophical implications for truth, morality, power, and the free spirit: The section deepens the critique of "ocular evidence" and popular sensualism while complicating any simple opposition between "appearance" and higher truth. Both modern natural science and Platonic philosophy are forms of interpretation and arrangement; they differ in taste, rank, and the kind of enjoyment or power they afford. The plebeian mode is persuasive to the many because it flatters the senses; the aristocratic mode achieves a higher triumph by mastering stronger senses through conceptual nets. This prepares the free spirit's task of conscious, experimental interpretation and the noble soul's capacity for distance and form-giving. It challenges both scientistic reduction ("only what can be seen and felt") and any uncritical return to metaphysics, insisting on the interpretive character of all world-engagement while preserving a distinction of rank between modes of interpretation. It advances BGE by linking epistemology to the problem of rank and nobility.

Modern relevance is immediate in debates over scientism, the limits of "data-driven" approaches, and the value of interpretive or humanistic disciplines in an age dominated by quantification and sensory/technological mediation. Nietzsche's contrast between plebeian sensualism and aristocratic resistance to the "mob of the senses" speaks to digital overload, algorithmic personalization of reality, and the question of whether "rough work" (engineering, optimization, bridge-building) exhausts the human task or whether higher pleasures of conceptual and aesthetic mastery remain possible and desirable. The section encourages suspicion of any claim that the world has been finally "explained" by what is most obvious to the senses or instruments, while recognizing that different modes of exposition serve different human types and projects.
Key Context: Part One of BGE. Returns to senses vs. interpretation theme; contrasts plebeian and aristocratic modes of philosophy; reinforces perspectivism and the role of rank/taste in knowledge. Bridges to later discussions of nobility, peoples, and what is noble.
