FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 14

Identifier: 14 • Natural Philosophy as Exposition • Part One
Simple modern summary

Science based on the senses is not explaining the world—it is arranging it in a way that feels convincing. Plato's approach rejected raw sense data and built elegant concepts on top of it. Both are interpretations; neither gets you to a final, objective truth.

Philosophical implications for truth, morality, power, and the free spirit: The section deepens the critique of "ocular evidence" and popular sensualism while complicating any simple opposition between "appearance" and higher truth. Both modern natural science and Platonic philosophy are forms of interpretation and arrangement; they differ in taste, rank, and the kind of enjoyment or power they afford. The plebeian mode is persuasive to the many because it flatters the senses; the aristocratic mode achieves a higher triumph by mastering stronger senses through conceptual nets. Key Context: Part One of BGE. Returns to senses vs. interpretation theme; contrasts plebeian and aristocratic modes of philosophy; reinforces perspectivism and the role of rank/taste in knowledge. Bridges to later discussions of nobility, peoples, and what is noble.
