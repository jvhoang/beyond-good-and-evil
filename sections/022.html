FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 22

Identifier: 22
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
"Nature's conformity to law," of which physicists talk so proudly, exists only owing to your interpretation and bad "philology." It is no matter of fact, no "text," but rather a naively humanitarian adjustment and perversion of meaning, with which you make abundant concessions to the democratic instincts of the modern soul. "Everywhere equality before the law—Nature is not different in that respect, nor better than we": a fine instance of secret motive in which vulgar antagonism to everything privileged and autocratic is disguised as science. "Ni dieu, ni maitre" — that, also, is what you want; and therefore "Cheers for natural law!"

But that is interpretation, not text. Somebody might come along with opposite intentions who could read out of the same "Nature" the tyrannically inconsiderate and relentless enforcement of the claims of power — an interpreter who places the unexceptionalness and unconditionalness of all "Will to Power" before your eyes. In the end this interpreter would assert the same about this world: that it has a "necessary" and "calculable" course, not because laws obtain in it, but because they are absolutely lacking, and every power effects its ultimate consequences every moment.

Key Takeaway: All 'laws of nature' are our interpretations and impositions. We are the law-givers. An alternative reading reveals will to power as the fundamental character of the world: not law but the unconditional discharge of power. Even this remains interpretation — and 'so much the better.'

Connections to the broader BGE project: Continues the philological and psychological critique of Part One. Exposes the democratic, anti-hierarchical bias hidden in scientific 'objectivity.' Prepares the positive 'will to power' psychology announced in the Preface and developed later. Shows that even the physicist's world-picture is a moral interpretation in disguise.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Pay attention to the ironic tone toward 'you physicists' and the 'modern soul.' The section is a masterclass in how to read 'Nature' as text with multiple possible interpretations. The final 'Granted that this also is only interpretation' is crucial — it prevents the will to power from hardening into a new dogma.

Why it matters today: In physics, biology, and data science, models and 'laws' are human constructs that fit data for prediction and control (power). Climate models, economic 'laws,' and algorithmic 'fairness' all carry interpretive and often democratic or egalitarian assumptions. Nietzsche invites us to ask what alternative interpretations (e.g., power dynamics, hierarchy, exception) our sciences suppress, and to own our role as interpreters rather than passive discoverers.
Companion Polish: This is perspectivism applied to science itself. The free spirit learns to see 'conformity to law' as one powerful but partial reading among others, and to value the honesty that admits 'this too is interpretation.'
