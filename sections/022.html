FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 22

Identifier: 22
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
“Nature’s conformity to law,” of which physicists talk so proudly, exists only owing to your interpretation and bad “philology.” It is no matter of fact, no “text,” but a naively humanitarian adjustment and perversion of meaning, with which you make abundant concessions to the democratic instincts of the modern soul. “Everywhere equality before the law—Nature is not different... nor better than we”: a fine instance of secret motive in which vulgar antagonism to everything privileged and autocratic (a second, more refined atheism) is disguised. “Ni dieu, ni maître”—that also is what you want; therefore “Cheers for natural law!”

Breakdown of the argument:
• This is interpretation, not text. Somebody might come along with opposite intentions and read out of the same Nature the tyrannically inconsiderate and relentless enforcement of the claims of power—an interpreter who places the unexceptionalness and unconditionalness of all “Will to Power” before your eyes.
• Almost every word, and the word “tyranny” itself, would seem unsuitable or a weakening metaphor—too human. Yet this interpreter would end by asserting the same about the world: that it has a “necessary” and “calculable” course, not because laws obtain, but because they are absolutely lacking, and every power effects its ultimate consequences every moment.
• Granted that this too is only interpretation—well, so much the better.

Key Takeaway: All talk of natural law is our creative imposition and tyranny via language. Science is our will to power in the guise of humility. The world is not law-governed; it is a chaos of powers whose calculability comes from the absence of exceptions to the will to power itself.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Continues the philological critique begun in earlier sections. Undermines the last refuge of metaphysical comfort in the “lawfulness” of nature. Directly feeds the will-to-power psychology that replaces both moral and scientific dogmatism. The free spirit learns to read Nature as text for power, not for democratic equality.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Nietzsche the old philologist is at his mischievous best here. Note the shift from physicists’ proud “conformity to law” to the possibility of reading the identical phenomena as unconditional will to power. The “so much the better” at the end is liberating.

Why it matters today: Modern physics and complexity science often still speak in the language of laws while acknowledging deep contingency and power dynamics. Social sciences and data-driven governance frequently impose “natural” regularities that are really our interpretive projections. Nietzsche gives permission to see our models as tools of power and to prefer the more honest, more terrible reading when it reveals the reality of power.
Companion Polish: This is one of Nietzsche’s most exhilarating passages on interpretation. The physicist and the will-to-power interpreter may describe the same “calculable course,” but only the latter has stopped lying to himself about whose will is at work.
