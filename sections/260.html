FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 260

Master Morality and Slave Morality
Identifier: 260
Simple modern summary

Every higher culture mixes two moralities: noble souls who create values from abundance, and reactive slave morality that levels through resentment and utility. Today's "progressive" values often show slave-moral traits (safety, pity, suspicion of strength). Recognizing both types in yourself and culture is step one toward creating something new.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
After touring the many moral systems that have ruled on earth, Nietzsche distills two primary types: MASTER-MORALITY and SLAVE-MORALITY. In higher and mixed civilizations these often appear in attempts at mediation, confusion, or even juxtaposition within a single soul.

Master Morality originates in a ruling caste, pleasantly conscious of its difference from the ruled. "Good" initially designates the exalted, proud, noble disposition; "bad" the despicable, cowardly, timid, insignificant, narrowly utilitarian, distrustful, self-abasing, dog-like, mendicant, and especially the untruthful (the common people are held to be untruthful; the Greek nobles called themselves "we truthful ones").

The noble type regards himself as a determiner of values; he does not require approval. He passes judgment: what is injurious to me is injurious in itself. He is a CREATOR OF VALUES. He honors in himself the powerful one, he who has power over himself, who speaks and keeps silence, who subjects himself to severity and hardness and reveres all that is severe and hard ("Wotan placed a hard heart in my breast"). He is proud of not being made for sympathy. Self-glorification, plenitude, the happiness of high tension, the overflowing wealth that gives and bestows — these define him. He helps the unfortunate not primarily from pity but from super-abundance of power. He has a radical enmity and irony toward "selflessness."

Duties exist only to one's equals; toward lower ranks one may act "beyond good and evil" as the heart desires. Prolonged gratitude and revenge are practiced within the circle of equals. The powerful know how to honor; reverence for age and tradition is fundamental. "Progress" and the "future" are suspect modern prejudices of ignoble origin.

Slave Morality originates among the oppressed, suffering, weary, and unfree. It expresses a pessimistic suspicion or condemnation of man's situation. The virtues of the powerful are viewed with an unfavorable, skeptical eye — their happiness is suspected of not being genuine. Instead, the qualities that alleviate suffering are exalted: sympathy, the kind helping hand, warm heart, patience, diligence, humility, friendliness. These are the most useful for the sufferers. Slave-morality is essentially the morality of utility.

Here arises the famous antithesis "good" and "evil." Evil is that which inspires fear — power, danger, dreadfulness, subtlety, strength. In slave-morality the "evil" man arouses fear; in master-morality it is precisely the "good" man who arouses and seeks to arouse fear, while the "bad" is merely despicable. Ultimately the "good" man of slave-morality becomes the safe man: good-natured, easily deceived, perhaps a little stupid — un bonhomme. Language itself tends to equate "good" and "stupid."

The desire for freedom, the instinct for happiness, and the refinements of the feeling of freedom belong to slave morality.

Key Insight: All higher cultures contain both moralities in tension or mixture. The noble soul creates values from its own plenitude and affirms distance and rank. Slave morality reacts against the noble, inverts values through utility and resentment, and levels. The philosopher of the future must understand this genealogy to create beyond both in a higher synthesis.

Modern companion insight: Contemporary "progressive" values often display classic slave-moral traits (emphasis on safety, utility, universal pity, suspicion of strength and excellence). Master-moral residues appear in competitive excellence, self-reverence, and the will to create new standards. Recognizing the two types within ourselves and culture is essential for any genuine revaluation.
