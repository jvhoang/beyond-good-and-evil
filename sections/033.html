FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 33

Identifier: 33 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
There is no other way: the feelings of devotion, self-sacrifice for one's neighbor, the whole morality of self-denial must be questioned mercilessly and taken to court — no less than the aesthetics of “contemplation devoid of all interest” which is used today as a seductive guise for the emasculation of art, to give it a good conscience. There is too much charm and sugar in these feelings of “for others,” “not for myself,” for us not to need to become doubly suspicious at this point and to ask: “are these not perhaps — seductions?” That they please those who have them and those who enjoy their fruits, and also the mere spectator — this does not yet constitute an argument in their favor but rather invites caution. So let us be cautious.

Key Takeaway: The morality of self-denial, pity, and “for others” is not self-evidently noble; its sweetness and popularity are reasons for suspicion. It may be a seduction that weakens life and art. The free spirit questions it mercilessly.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This is a direct assault on the altruistic, Christian, and Schopenhauerian values that the book seeks to overcome. It links the critique of morality (Part One) to the positive project of the free spirit who affirms his own strength rather than self-sacrifice. It prepares the revaluation and the “beyond good and evil” stance.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The section is short and sharp. The repetition of “caution” and the question “are these not perhaps — seductions?” is the core. Note how it groups moral self-denial with the aesthetic ideal of disinterested contemplation — both are targets.

Why it matters today: Contemporary culture is saturated with the morality of self-denial in the form of performative altruism, virtue-signaling, corporate social responsibility theater, and the demand that art and thought serve “social justice” or be “for others.” Nietzsche’s suspicion remains vital: these feelings are pleasant to hold and to applaud, but they may serve as seductions that emasculate strength, honesty, and genuine creation. The free spirit learns to ask of every “good” feeling: Cui bono? What type does this strengthen or weaken?
Companion Polish: Charm and sugar are not arguments for a value. The free spirit cultivates the strength to be suspicious precisely where the culture finds the greatest moral consensus and emotional satisfaction.
