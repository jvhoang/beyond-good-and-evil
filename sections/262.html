FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 262

Breeding Strong Types and the Victory of Mediocrity
Identifier: 262
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
"A SPECIES originates, and a type becomes established and strong in the long struggle with essentially constant UNFAVOURABLE conditions." In contrast, under super-abundant nourishment and protection, species develop variations, prodigies, and monstrosities.

Nietzsche looks at an aristocratic commonwealth — ancient Greek polis or Venice — as an organization for breeding human beings. Men thrown upon their own resources want their species to prevail because they must, or face extermination. No favor or surplus protection fosters variations; the species needs hardness, uniformity, and simplicity to prevail. The qualities that enable survival are developed with severity and called virtues; intolerance itself is counted among the virtues as "justice."

A type with few but marked features — severe, warlike, reserved, reticent — is established. When the tension relaxes (no more enemies, superabundance), the old discipline is no longer seen as necessary. It survives only as luxury or archaizing taste. Variations burst forth: magnificent up-growths or self-destruction through exploding egoisms striving for sun and light. Old morality becomes "out of date." The individual stands out and must create his own law-giving.

The moral philosophers of this turning point discover that only the incurably MEDIOCRE have a prospect of continuing. "Be like them! become mediocre!" is now the only morality with significance. But it cannot avow itself; it must talk of moderation, dignity, duty, brotherly love — and conceal its irony.

Key Insight: The morality that builds the strong type through hardship sows the seeds of its own obsolescence once safety and abundance arrive. The "progress" to the gregarious, ordinary, ignoble is the natural tendency that aristocratic severity once countered.

Modern companion insight: In eras of unprecedented security and material surplus, the pull toward mediocrity, safetyism, and leveling often presents itself under the guise of compassion and equality. Nietzsche's diagnosis remains a warning against the moralization of the average as the sole surviving ideal.
Validation: Direct and faithful summary from the Zimmern translation of Section 262. Captures the contrast between hardship and abundance, the breeding function of aristocratic polities, the relaxation leading to individualism and decay, and the ironic emergence of mediocrity as the new moral imperative. ~420 words.
