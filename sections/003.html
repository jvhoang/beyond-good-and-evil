FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 3

Identifier: 3
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Having watched philosophers closely and “read between their lines long enough,” Nietzsche concludes that the greater part of conscious thinking—even philosophical thinking—must be counted among the instinctive functions. “Being-conscious” is not opposed to instinct in any decisive sense. Behind all logic and its seeming sovereignty lie valuations, or, more plainly, “physiological demands for the maintenance of a definite mode of life.”

Certainty is preferred over uncertainty, illusion over truth, not because one is objectively superior but because such valuations serve beings like us. Supposing, Nietzsche adds with a sting, “that man is not just the ‘measure of things.’”

Key Insight for the book: The philosopher is not a pure intellect contemplating eternal verities. He is a living creature whose most refined thoughts are secretly shaped by the needs of his particular mode of life.

How it connects: This naturalizes the “will to truth” questioned in Section 1 and the free spirit of the Preface. It undercuts the pretensions of rationalist morality and prepares the psychological genealogies that follow throughout BGE.

Why it matters today: Modern science (evolutionary psychology, embodied cognition, cognitive bias research) has largely confirmed Nietzsche’s suspicion. Our “rational” arguments—political, moral, scientific—are often post-hoc justifications for deeper drives. The honest thinker must learn to read the instincts behind even his own most cherished certainties.
Companion Note: The philosopher’s most refined thoughts are physiological demands in disguise. Read every system of philosophy as a symptom of the body that produced it.
