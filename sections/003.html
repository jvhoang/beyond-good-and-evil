FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 3

Identifier: 3
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Having observed philosophers closely and “read between their lines long enough,” Nietzsche reaches a disquieting conclusion: the greater part of conscious thinking—even in philosophy—belongs among the instinctive functions. “Being-conscious” is not decisively opposed to instinct. Behind all logic and its apparent sovereignty lie valuations—more plainly, “physiological demands for the maintenance of a definite mode of life.”

We prefer certainty to uncertainty, illusion to truth, not because one is objectively truer but because these preferences help maintain a particular kind of life. “Supposing, in effect, that man is not just the ‘measure of things.’” The philosopher is therefore not a disembodied intellect contemplating eternal verities. He is a living creature whose most refined thoughts are secretly shaped by the needs and drives of his particular mode of existence.

Key Insight: All philosophy is, in part, a symptom of the body and its conditions of life. Conscious reasoning is often a post-hoc rationalization of deeper physiological and instinctual demands. The “impulse to knowledge” is rarely the father of a philosophy; another impulse has usually made use of knowledge (and mistaken knowledge) as its instrument.

Connection to the book: This section naturalizes the will to truth questioned in Section 1 and the free spirit invoked in the Preface. It undercuts any claim to pure rationalist morality and sets up the genealogical, psychological analyses that run through the rest of BGE (including the confessions of philosophers in Section 6 and the will as multiplicity in Section 19). Thought is never neutral; it serves life—or a particular form of it.

Why it matters: Evolutionary psychology, embodied cognition, and cognitive-bias research have largely vindicated Nietzsche’s suspicion. Our most “rational” political, moral, and scientific arguments are frequently post-hoc justifications for deeper drives. For readers of the Kaufmann translation, the honest thinker learns to read the instincts operating behind even his or her own most treasured certainties—and to ask what mode of life a philosophy (including Nietzsche’s) is serving.
Companion Note: The philosopher’s most refined thoughts are physiological demands in disguise. Read every system of philosophy—including this one—as a symptom of the body and culture that produced it. The slides here dramatize the hidden instincts pulling the strings of conscious logic; use them as a prompt to examine your own thinking while reading Kaufmann.
