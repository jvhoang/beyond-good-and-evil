FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 263

The Instinct for Rank as the Mark of Nobility
Identifier: 263
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
"There is an INSTINCT FOR RANK, which more than anything else is already the sign of a HIGH rank; there is a DELIGHT in the NUANCES of reverence which leads one to infer noble origin and habits."

The refinement, goodness, and loftiness of a soul are put to a perilous test when something of the highest rank passes by without the protection of authority — a living touchstone, tentative, perhaps veiled. The psychologist uses this to determine the ultimate value and innate order of rank of a soul.

"DIFFERENCE ENGENDRE HAINE": the vulgar soul spurts up like dirty water when a holy vessel, jewel from closed shrines, or book of great destiny is brought before it. In contrast, there is involuntary silence, hesitation of the eye, cessation of gestures when a soul feels the nearness of what is worthiest of respect.

The reverence for the BIBLE maintained in Europe is the best example of discipline and refinement owed to Christianity: profound books require external tyranny of authority to acquire the millennia needed to unriddle them. It is an advance toward humanity when the masses learn there are holy experiences before which one must take off one's shoes.

By contrast, in the cultured classes, believers in "modern ideas," nothing is so repulsive as their lack of shame, the easy insolence with which they touch, taste, and finger everything. There may still be more relative nobility of taste among peasants than among the newspaper-reading demimonde of intellect.

Key Insight: The instinct for rank and the capacity for reverence are themselves the highest signs of rank. Vulgarity reveals itself in the inability to withhold the unclean hand; true nobility in the spontaneous awe before the rare and worthy.

Modern companion insight: Contemporary culture of instant access, hot takes, and irreverent commentary on everything often celebrates the very lack of tact and reverence that Nietzsche identifies as plebeian. The free spirit cultivates the discipline to withhold touch.
Validation: Faithful rendering of Section 263's central theme of rank-instinct, the test of reverence, the contrast between vulgar insolence and noble hesitation, and the historical example of Biblical authority. ~340 words.
