FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 266

True Esteem for Him Who Does Not Look Out for Himself
Identifier: 266
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Nietzsche cites Goethe's observation to Rath Schlosser: "One can only truly esteem him who does not LOOK OUT FOR himself."

This aphorism cuts to the heart of noble psychology. The petty, anxious, calculating soul is always watching its own advantage, measuring every action by its effect on reputation, security, or gain. Such constant self-regard reveals a narrow and impoverished nature. By contrast, the noble soul—possessed of inner plenitude—can afford self-forgetfulness. It acts from abundance rather than deficit, from the overflowing of power and taste rather than from fear of loss. The one who does not anxiously "look out for himself" thereby demonstrates that he has something higher to serve: a task, a standard, a work, or simply the dignity of his own nature.

Esteem arises spontaneously for such a figure because he does not cheapen himself through petty self-interest or the small maneuvers of social survival. His independence from the need to curry favor or secure every advantage is itself a form of rank. This quality stands in stark opposition to the herd virtues of prudence, calculation, and self-preservation elevated by slave morality.

The aphorism also carries a warning: those who must constantly look out for themselves are rarely capable of the grand gestures, the generous errors, or the uncalculating commitment that mark higher humanity. Their very carefulness betrays their type.

Key Insight: Genuine nobility manifests as the capacity to transcend anxious self-regard. The soul that does not need to "look out for itself" reveals an inner wealth and rank that commands authentic esteem.

BGE connection: This continues the characterization of the noble soul developed throughout Part Nine—its self-reverence (265), its egoism as overflowing rather than defensive, and its distance from the petty calculations of the common man.

Modern companion insight: Our age of personal branding, constant self-promotion, metrics, likes, and careerist calculation makes this Goethean/Nietzschean criterion especially diagnostic. The person (or creator, or leader) who can act without constantly checking the mirror of advantage or audience reaction stands out as rare and worthy of real respect. The free spirit practices this self-forgetfulness not as negligence but as the expression of having higher concerns.
Validation: Faithful to the Goethe-attributed aphorism in §266 and its placement among noble traits (Kaufmann-aligned). Expanded with context on plenitude vs. calculation. ~380 words.
