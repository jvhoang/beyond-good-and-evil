FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 16

Identifier: 16 • No Immediate Certainties • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
In the broader context of Friedrich Nietzsche's Beyond Good and Evil, Section 16 extends Part One's relentless attack on the prejudices of philosophers. Having questioned the will to truth, value-antitheses, the instinctive character of thought, and the causal status of sense-organs, Nietzsche now targets the lingering superstition of "immediate certainties" — the Cartesian "I think" and Schopenhauer's "I will." This is central to the book's epistemological and psychological deconstruction, paving the way for the free spirit who lives without such foundations and for the doctrine of will to power as a more honest description of our drives.

The core argument exposes "I think" (and similar claims) as anything but immediate or certain. There are still "harmless self-observers who believe that there are 'immediate certainties'; for instance, 'I think,' or as the superstition of Schopenhauer puts it, 'I will'; as though cognition here got hold of its object purely and simply as 'the thing in itself,' without any falsification taking place either on the part of the subject or the object." Nietzsche insists that "immediate certainty," "absolute knowledge," and "the thing in itself" involve a CONTRADICTIO IN ADJECTO. Analyzing the sentence "I think" reveals a whole series of daring, unproven assertions: that it is I who think, that there must be something that thinks, that thinking is an activity of a causal being, that there is an "ego," and that we already know what thinking is. The philosopher finds instead "a series of metaphysical questions... veritable conscience questions of the intellect."

Key quotes:
"There are still harmless self-observers who believe that there are 'immediate certainties'; for instance, 'I think,' or as the superstition of Schopenhauer puts it, 'I will'; as though cognition here got hold of its object purely and simply as 'the thing in itself,' without any falsification taking place either on the part of the subject or the object."
"'immediate certainty,' as well as 'absolute knowledge' and the 'thing in itself,' involve a CONTRADICTIO IN ADJECTO"
"When I analyze the process that is expressed in the sentence, 'I think,' I find a whole series of daring assertions..."

Philosophical implications for truth/morality/power/free spirit: The unified, transparent "I" or subject is shown to be a grammatical and metaphysical fiction, not a given certainty. All cognition involves comparison, interpretation, and falsification; there is no direct access to "the thing in itself." This undermines Cartesian foundationalism and any morality or metaphysics built upon a simple, self-transparent agent or soul. For the free spirit and will to power, power and knowledge arise from complex, interested processes rather than from a sovereign "I." It advances BGE by removing another dogmatic "beyond" — the immediate self — and inviting a more rigorous, philological, and physiological psychology.
Key Context: Part One of BGE. Emphasizes perspectivism, the critique of the subject/ego, and the will to power against foundationalist certainties.
