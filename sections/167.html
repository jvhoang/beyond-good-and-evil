FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 167

Intimacy: Shame and Treasure for the Vigorous
Identifier: 167
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
“To vigorous men intimacy is a matter of shame—and something precious.”

For those of strong, self-sufficient nature, closeness with others is double-edged. It exposes vulnerability that the vigorous instinctively resist; hence the element of shame. At the same time, genuine intimacy is rare and therefore highly valued—something to be guarded rather than squandered.

Nietzsche contrasts this with weaker types who may seek intimacy indiscriminately or treat it lightly. The strong experience both the cost (loss of autonomy, exposure) and the worth (rare connection worthy of protection) more acutely. Intimacy for them is not casual companionship but a significant concession of distance.

The observation belongs to Nietzsche’s broader psychology of rank: different types experience the same phenomena at different intensities and valuations. What is precious to one may be cheap or dangerous to another.

Key Insight: Strength entails a heightened sense of boundaries. The vigorous man does not easily grant access to his inner world; when he does, the act carries weight precisely because it is difficult.

Modern companion insight: In a culture that prizes radical transparency and constant sharing, the vigorous type’s reserve can seem cold or elitist. Yet the epigram reminds us that easy accessibility often signals either weakness or lack of anything substantial to protect. True intimacy remains rare and costly; the free spirit neither hoards nor dissipates it indiscriminately.
Key Context: Epigram on types of character, boundaries, and the psychology of closeness. Part Four.
