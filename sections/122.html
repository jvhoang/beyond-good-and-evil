FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 122

Politeness of Heart
Identifier: 122
Simple modern summary

Enjoying praise is often just heart-politeness, not vanity. Warm gratitude toward the giver is not the same as ego obsession. Do not confuse courtesy with narcissism.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

This section looks at why humans chase certainty and how our deepest drives shape what we call 'truth'.

Key insight

Genuine delight in being praised is often not the mark of a vain or self-absorbed character but rather a generous, warm-hearted response. It's courteous to the giver of praise. True vanity of spirit would be something colder, more calculating, more concerned with the enhancement of one’s image regardless of the sincerity of the source. The distinction separates warm social feeling from spiritual self-importance. Not all pleasure in recognition is pathological. Politeness of heart — simple, uncalculating gratitude or pleasure in another’s good opinion — can be a sign of health and nobility rather than weakness or vanity.

BGE connection

This refines the psychology of the free spirit and the noble soul. The free spirit is not above all affects or social feelings. higher types can afford warm responses without being enslaved by them. It also continues the critique of moralists who pathologize every natural pleasure. rethinking of values involves distinguishing healthy expressions of self-affirmation from the twisted forms produced by morality of the weak and ressentiment.

Modern companion

In 2026’s culture of performative humility, “cringe” avoidance, and constant suspicion of anyone who appears to enjoy success or recognition, this aphorism is refreshing. Many people have internalized the idea that any visible pleasure in praise is suspect or narcissistic. The result is a culture of ironic self-deprecation that can mask actual vanity while punishing healthy warmth. The free spirit can receive praise with simple pleasure and gratitude without immediately performing elaborate disclaimers or turning the moment into a status play. At the same time, one remains alert to the difference between warm-hearted reception and the deeper spiritual vanity that hungers for admiration as fuel for a fragile ego. Warmth is not the same as neediness. Distinguishing the two is an art of the higher soul.

Key Context: Epigram distinguishing healthy social feeling from spiritual vanity in Part Four. Refines the moral psychology of self-regard and recognition.
