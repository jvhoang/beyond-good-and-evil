FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 127

Science Hostile to Woman's Shame
Identifier: 127
Simple modern summary

Science feels hostile to women's sense of shame—like peeping under skin or clothes. The investigative gaze can read as violation, not neutral curiosity. Truth-seeking threatens protective modesty.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 127 states: “In the eyes of all true women science is hostile to the sense of shame. They feel as if one wished to peep under their skin with it—or worse still! under their dress and finery.”

Science’s ruthless drive to uncover, expose, and analyze strikes many women (in Nietzsche’s formulation) as an intimate violation. The “true woman” experiences the scientific gaze not as neutral curiosity but as an undressing that strips protective layers of shame, modesty, and self-veiling. Shame here is not mere embarrassment but a form of dignity, boundary, and depth that science’s will to truth threatens to penetrate.

Key Insight: The passion for knowledge can register as aggression against the protective instincts and veils that sustain certain forms of life and selfhood. Not all truth-seeking feels like liberation.

Connection to BGE themes: The epigram contributes to Nietzsche’s psychology of the sexes, his analysis of the will to truth, and his suspicion that truth at any price may be a disguised form of cruelty or ressentiment. It sits alongside other Part Four reflections on appearance, depth, and the value of surfaces. The free spirit must ask whether every veil should be torn and whether some forms of knowledge require or deserve resistance. Revaluation includes questioning the moral monopoly claimed by the scientific unmasker.

Modern companion insight: In 2026 debates around data surveillance, gender medicine, social media exposure, genetic testing, and “transparency” culture echo this tension. Many women experience certain scientific, journalistic, or technological inquiries into bodies, psychology, or behavior as invasive rather than empowering. At the same time, movements for bodily autonomy and privacy push back against institutional “peeping.” The free spirit navigates without dogmatism: neither worshiping every disclosure as progress nor romanticizing ignorance, but recognizing that shame can be a sophisticated defense mechanism and that the cost of certain knowledges falls unevenly. Intellectual honesty includes acknowledging when the drive for truth functions as undressing rather than enlightenment. Not every secret benefits from being dragged into the light.

Some veils exist for reasons that calculation cannot see.
Key Context: Epigram on science, shame, gender, and the limits of the will to truth in Part Four. Extends the critique of naive enlightenment and exposure.
