FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 27

Identifier: 27 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
It is hard to be understood, especially when one thinks and lives *gangasrotogati* among men who think and live differently—namely, *kurmagati*, or at best “the way frogs walk,” *mandeikagati* (I obviously do everything to be “hard to understand” myself!)—and one should be cordially grateful for the good will to some subtlety of interpretation.

As regards “the good friends,” however, who are always too lazy and think that as friends they have a right to relax, one does well to grant them from the outset some leeway and romping place for misunderstanding: then one can even laugh—or get rid of them altogether, these good friends—and also laugh.

Key Takeaway: The free spirit who moves with the swift, flowing tempo of the Ganges (gangasrotogati) among tortoise-paced or frog-walking contemporaries must expect to be misunderstood. Subtle interpreters are rare and precious; “good friends” who claim the right to lazy misunderstanding should be given rope or cheerfully discarded. The philosopher cultivates both gratitude for rare subtlety and laughter at the inevitable.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Continues the theme of strategic concealment and independence (25–26). The free spirit’s difference in tempo and depth makes ordinary sociality costly; masks, solitude, and selective relationships are necessary. Prepares the more radical solitude and experimentalism of later sections in Part Two.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The Sanskrit tempo metaphors are a delight and a challenge. Nietzsche is describing his own stylistic and intellectual speed. The parenthetical “(I obviously do everything to be 'hard to understand' myself!)” is pure self-aware mischief.

Why it matters today: In a culture of instant takes, 280-character discourse, and the demand that every idea be immediately accessible and “relatable,” the swift, deep current of genuine thought will always be misread. Nietzsche gives permission to value subtlety over popularity and to curate one’s circle ruthlessly, including letting “good friends” go with laughter rather than resentment.
Companion Polish: A short, sparkling aphorism on the social cost of intellectual tempo. Laughter is the free spirit’s best defense and hygiene.
