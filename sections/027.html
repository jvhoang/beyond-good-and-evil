FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 27

Identifier: 27 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
Simple modern summary

If you think fast and deeply, most people will misunderstand you—and that is normal. Nietzsche says lazy friends who want easy answers are not worth the effort. Better to let them misread you, laugh, or move on.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

This section looks at why humans chase certainty and how our deepest drives shape what we call 'truth'.

Main points
  • This section explores how our thinking is shaped by hidden drives.
  • We often value certainty and comfort more than uncomfortable truth.
  • In simple modern terms, this shows that people (and whole systems of thought) often disguise personal biases, fears, or power interests as pure objective truth — so we should stay alert to the real motives behind big ideas, including our own.
Key Kaufmann quotes
Key Kaufmann quotes
  • “It is hard to be understood, especially when one thinks and lives *gangasrotogati* among men who think and live differently – namely, kurmagati, or at best 'the way frogs walk,' *mandeikagati* (I obviously do everything to be 'hard to understand' myself!)...”
  • “As regards 'the good friends,' however, who are always too lazy and think that as friends they have a right to relax, one does well to grant them from the outset some leeway and romping place for misunderstanding...”
  • “the way frogs walk,”

Philosophical implications

The free spirit's communication is inherently perspectival and tempo-dependent. misunderstanding is inevitable and can be strategically embraced or used to filter true companions. Power lies in deliberate difficulty and laughter at the lazy. This advances BGE's critique of dogmatism by rejecting the ideal of transparent, popular, or "friendly" communication that dogmatists often seek. the free spirit values subtlety over accessibility and is willing to lose "good friends" for the sake of integrity.

Key Context: Brief interlude on style and friendship in the free spirit's life. Ties to tempo (28) and independence (29).
