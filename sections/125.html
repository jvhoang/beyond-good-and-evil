FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 125

Changing Opinion and Its Inconvenience
Identifier: 125
Simple modern summary

When we have to change our mind about someone, we blame them for the inconvenience. Updating beliefs costs effort, so we resent the person who forced it. Ego hates the homework of being wrong.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Main points
  • What is called spirit seeks mastery and growth, not disinterested knowledge.
  • Its goal is the feeling of increased power, including the will to appearance and simplification.
  • Knowledge and philosophy are expressions of this drive, not pure or neutral.
  • In simple modern terms, this shows that people (and whole systems of thought) often disguise personal biases, fears, or power interests as pure objective truth — so we should stay alert to the real motives behind big ideas, including our own.
Context

The epigram diagnoses a common but rarely acknowledged mental accounting.

Key insight

The epigram diagnoses a common but rarely acknowledged mental accounting. Revising a judgment about another person requires cognitive effort, emotional recalibration, and often the rewriting of personal or social narratives. Rather than accept the cost as the price of accuracy, we debit it to the character of the person who forced the revision. The resentment is not primarily toward the content of the new view but toward the labor of arriving at it. The “inconvenience” becomes a moral charge. Much interpersonal hostility arises not from substantive disagreement but from the ego’s refusal to pay the bill for updating its files. We prefer to keep faulty opinions than admit the inconvenience of correction.

BGE connection

Nietzsche’s free spirit is defined by lightness of opinion and the capacity for swift, unresentful revision. This aphorism exposes the psychological obstacle that keeps most people tethered to outdated views. It belongs to the book’s ongoing unmasking of how drives and petty self-preservation masquerade as judgment. rethinking of values cannot proceed if every correction of perspective is experienced as a personal injury inflicted by reality or by others. The epigram thus supports the larger call for intellectual honesty and the noble capacity to be wrong without vengeance.

Modern companion

In 2026 the phenomenon is amplified by constant information flow and identity-tied beliefs. Changing one’s mind about a public figure, a news story, or a personal acquaintance on social media often incurs social costs within one’s tribe. Platforms reward consistency over accuracy. admitting error is framed as weakness or betrayal. People therefore “charge heavily” to the source of new evidence. Professional contexts, romantic relationships, and political affiliations all exhibit the same pattern: the inconvenience of updating is projected outward. The free spirit in the present age cultivates the opposite habit — welcoming the small daily corrections, keeping separate the pain of mental work from the worth of the person who prompted it, and treating revised opinions as ordinary hygiene rather than moral drama. Accurate maps require frequent redrawing. billing the cartographer for the eraser is a costly superstition. We resent the messenger of our own necessary revisions.

Key Context: Epigram on the psychology of belief revision and interpersonal resentment in Part Four. Illuminates obstacles to the free spirit’s perspectival agility.
