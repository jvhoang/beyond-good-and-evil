FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 31

Identifier: 31 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
When one is young, one venerates and despises without that art of nuances which constitutes the best gain of life, and it is only fair that one has to pay dearly for having assaulted men and things in this manner with Yes and No. Everything is arranged so that the worst of tastes, the taste for the unconditional, should be cruelly fooled and abused until a man learns to put a little art into his feelings and rather to risk trying even what is artificial—as the real artists of life do.

The wrathful and reverent attitudes characteristic of youth do not seem to permit themselves any rest until they have forged men and things in such a way that these attitudes may be vented on them—after all, youth in itself has something of forgery and deception. Later, when the young soul, tortured by all kinds of disappointments, finally turns suspiciously against itself, still hot and wild, even in its suspicion and pangs of conscience—how wroth it is with itself now! how it tears itself to pieces, impatiently! how it takes revenge for its long self-delusion, just as if it had been a deliberate blindness! In this transition one punishes oneself with mistrust against one’s own feelings; one tortures one’s own enthusiasm with doubts; indeed, one experiences even a good conscience as a danger, as if it were a way of wrapping oneself in veils and the exhaustion of subtler honesty—and above all one takes sides, takes sides on principle, against “youth.” Ten years later one comprehends that all this, too—was still youth.

Key Takeaway: Youth is characterized by unconditional Yes/No, forgery, and the absence of nuance. Maturation involves learning the art of nuance, risking the artificial, and eventually recognizing even one’s hard-won suspicion and self-torture as still another phase of youth. The “best gain of life” is the subtle art that replaces crude veneration and contempt.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This psychological portrait of development prepares the free spirit who has passed through unconditional moralism, disappointment, and self-suspicion into a lighter, more artistic relation to values and to himself. It echoes the Preface’s tension and the need for free spirits strong enough not to need new certainties.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The progression from youthful forgery to self-lacerating suspicion to the quiet recognition “Ten years later one comprehends that all this, too—was still youth” is one of the most moving and accurate descriptions of intellectual growing up in Nietzsche.

Why it matters today: In an age of performative certainty on social media, cancel culture’s binary moralism, and the pressure to have fully formed opinions immediately, this section is medicine. It normalizes the long, painful, and often ridiculous process of developing genuine nuance and the right to ambivalence.
Companion Polish: A compassionate and ruthless account of how one actually acquires the art of life. Even the proudest skepticism was once youth.
