Section 111: Our Vanity Is Most Difficult to Wound

Part Four: Epigrams and Interludes • Beyond Good and Evil

Simple modern summary

Vanity is hardest to wound exactly when pride just got hit. A wounded ego armors itself against the usual flattery. The deeper the pride wound, the less vanity cares about praise.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

Nietzsche's sharp epigram: "Our vanity is most difficult to wound just when our pride has been wounded."

Key insight

When pride is struck, the usual channels of vanity (desire for admiration, recognition, superiority) are disrupted or inverted. The wound to pride makes one less susceptible to the usual flattery or social stroking that vanity craves—perhaps because the ego retreats into a defensive posture or because the injury makes external validation ring hollow. Vanity, normally porous and hungry, becomes armored precisely at the moment of deepest injury.

Key Kaufmann quotes

Key Kaufmann context in Part Four: these interludes probe the psychology of self-regard, masks, and the subtle ways the soul protects itself. The free spirit must notice such mechanisms in self and others without moral judgment—understanding vanity's resilience as a datum of human nature rather than a simple flaw to condemn.

Modern companion

In social media age of public shaming and status wounds, the epigram explains why the proudest or most status-conscious may shrug off superficial compliments or attacks after a real hit to self-image. It invites revaluation: perhaps cultivating a pride deep enough that vanity loses its power altogether is part of nobility. ~210 words. Direct from the epigram and context in Kaufmann translation.
