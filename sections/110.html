Advocates of the Criminal

Part Four: Epigrams and Interludes • Beyond Good and Evil

CONCISE SUMMARY (Faithful to Epigram)

Nietzsche observes that "The advocates of a criminal are seldom artists enough to turn the beautiful terribleness of the deed to the advantage of the doer." Defenders, lawyers, or sympathizers rarely possess the aesthetic or psychological power to reframe a criminal act as something grand, terrible, and life-affirming in its own right. Instead, they typically resort to mitigation, pity, or denial, robbing the deed (and the doer) of its potential tragic or heroic dimension.

This epigram extends the previous one (109): not only does the criminal himself often fail to equal his deed, but those who speak for him compound the diminishment. True "advocacy" in Nietzsche's sense would require an artist of psychology capable of affirming the deed's terribleness without moralistic softening—turning it into a source of strength or beauty for the actor. Most "advocates" (including modern reformers, psychologists, or moralists) lack this; they humanize downward.

Implication for the free spirit and "beyond good and evil": the capacity to see and affirm the "beautiful terribleness" of actions—whether one's own or others'—without the need for extenuation. This is part of revaluing values and the artistic interpretation of life. The epigram critiques both punitive and overly sentimental approaches to "crime." In today's polarized debates over justice and victimhood, it challenges us to ask whether our narratives serve truth and strength or merely comfort. ~205 words.
