FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 35

Identifier: 35 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
O Voltaire! O humaneness! O nonsense! There is something about “truth,” about the search for truth; and when a human being is too human about it—“il ne cherche le vrai que pour faire le bien”—I bet he finds nothing.

Key Takeaway: The search for truth undertaken from motives of humaneness, philanthropy, or the desire to “do good” is self-defeating and finds nothing of value. Voltairean enlightenment, when it becomes sentimental and moralistic, degenerates into nonsense. The genuine seeker must be harder, less “human,” more willing to risk findings that are not comfortable or beneficial in the ordinary sense.

Connections to the broader BGE project: A sharp jab at the optimistic, moralizing strand of the Enlightenment (Voltaire as emblem). It aligns with the critique of “good Europeans” who still carry too much Christian/humanitarian sugar and with the call for free spirits strong enough for dangerous, non-consoling truths. Prepares the harder, more aristocratic stance of later sections.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The exclamation is both affectionate and mocking. The French phrase (“he seeks the true only in order to do the good”) is the precise target. Nietzsche bets against the moralized truth-seeker.

Why it matters today: Much contemporary “truth-seeking” in journalism, academia, and activism is explicitly framed as serving social justice or humaneness. Nietzsche predicts (and we see) that such motivated inquiry reliably produces comforting narratives rather than hard truths. The free spirit searches without the prior commitment to “doing good.”
Companion Polish: A one-sentence demolition of moralized enlightenment. Truth does not come with a humanitarian warranty.
