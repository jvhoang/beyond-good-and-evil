FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 32

Identifier: 32 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
During the longest part of human history—so-called prehistorical times—the value or disvalue of an action was derived from its consequences. The action itself was considered as little as its origin. It was rather the retroactive force of success or failure that led men to think well or ill of an action. Let us call this period the pre-moral period of mankind: the imperative “know thyself!” was as yet unknown.

In the last ten thousand years, however, one has reached the point, step by step, in a few large regions on the earth, where it is no longer the consequences but the origin of an action that one allows to decide its value. On the whole this is a great event which involves a considerable refinement of vision and standards; it is the unconscious aftereffect of the rule of aristocratic values and the faith in “descent”—the sign of a period that one may call moral in the narrower sense. It involves the first attempt at self-knowledge. Instead of the consequences, the origin: indeed a reversal of perspective!

Surely, a reversal achieved only after long struggles and vacillations. To be sure, a calamitous new superstition, an odd narrowness of interpretation, thus become dominant: the origin of an action was interpreted in the most definite sense as origin in an intention; one came to agree that the value of an action lay in the value of the intention. The intention as the whole origin and prehistory of an action—almost to the present day this prejudice dominated moral praise, blame, judgment, and philosophy on earth.

But today—shouldn’t we have reached the necessity of once more resolving on a reversal and fundamental shift in values, owing to another self-examination of man, another growth in profundity? Don’t we stand at the threshold of a period which should be designated negatively, to begin with, as extra-moral?

Key Takeaway: Mankind has moved from a pre-moral stage (value in consequences) to a moral stage (value in intentions). We are now at the threshold of an extra-moral stage that goes beyond both, returning to a deeper, more profound evaluation that no longer rests on the fiction of the intending subject as the sole origin of value.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This is the historical-psychological backbone for the entire revaluation of values. It explains why “morality” in the narrower sense must be overcome. The will-to-power psychology and the free spirit’s task belong to the coming extra-moral period.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): This is one of the clearest statements of Nietzsche’s genealogical method and his periodization of morality. Note the calm, almost scientific tone giving way to the prophetic “Don’t we stand at the threshold...”

Why it matters today: Contemporary ethics and law still largely operate with the “intention” as the decisive factor (mens rea, good will, authentic self-expression). Nietzsche invites us to a post-intentional, extra-moral evaluation grounded in drives, consequences at a deeper level, and the rank of the actor—preparing the ground for a genuinely post-Christian, post-liberal morality.
Companion Polish: A compact genealogy of morality in three stages. The “extra-moral” is not immoral; it is the next, more profound self-examination.
