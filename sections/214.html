Section 214: Our Virtues?

Part Seven: Our Virtues • Beyond Good and Evil

Simple modern summary

Nietzsche asks whether modern Europeans can still have virtues when they are curious, divided, disguised, and sharp-edged. Any virtues we keep must fit our actual instincts, not our grandparents' simple codes. New people need new virtues.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY

Section 214 opens Part Seven by asking whether “we Europeans of the day-after-tomorrow” still have virtues, though they will not be the naive, four-square virtues of our grandfathers. With our dangerous curiosity, multiplicity, art of disguise, and “sweetened cruelty in spirit and sense,” any virtues we possess will be those that best tolerate our most secret inclinations and burning needs.

We must seek them in our labyrinths. The phrase “believe in one’s own virtues” is essentially the old “good conscience,” the worthy pigtail our grandfathers wore. In this one respect we remain their grandsons: we last Europeans with good consciences still carry that pigtail. Yet “Alas, if you knew how soon, how very soon—things will be otherwise!”

Key Insight: Our virtues, if any, will be subtle accommodations to our complex, experimental, post-moral natures rather than inherited absolutes. The old good conscience is already an anachronism.

Modern companion: Contemporary moral discourse still appeals to inherited “values” while lived ethics have become highly individualized, situational, and often ironic. The free spirit examines which of his drives and needs have genuinely found virtuous expression and which merely wear old masks. The warning is clear: the old pigtail is about to be cut; new forms of self-accounting are already arriving.
