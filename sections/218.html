FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART SEVEN

Section 218

The Rule Against the Exception: Study the Mediocrity
Identifier: 218
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Nietzsche directs psychologists—especially the French—to a neglected spectacle: “The psychologists of France... have never yet exhausted their bitter and manifold enjoyment of the betise bourgeoise... I would now recommend for a change something else for a pleasure—namely, the unconscious astuteness with which good, fat, honest mediocrity always behaves towards loftier spirits and the tasks they have to perform, the subtle, barbed, Jesuitical astuteness... In short, you psychologists, study the philosophy of the 'rule' in its struggle with the 'exception': there you have a spectacle fit for Gods and godlike malignity! Or, in plainer words, practise vivisection on 'good people,' on the 'homo bonae voluntatis,' ON YOURSELVES!”

The “good people,” the average, the rule, do not merely fail to understand the exception; they work instinctively, with refined cruelty, to relax, undermine, and neutralize higher types.

Key Insight: Mediocrity possesses its own cunning intelligence—the Jesuitism of the rule—which is deployed against everything that would rise above it. The proper study of the psychologist is not the criminal or the deviant but the “good” person’s war on the exceptional.

BGE connection: Closes the sequence in Part Seven that began with modern virtues and multiplicity. Having diagnosed our subtle, motley, dancing moralities, Nietzsche reveals the social mechanism that keeps them from producing anything truly higher: the instinctive leveling performed by the respectable many.

Modern companion insight: Democratic and egalitarian cultures institutionalize this vivisection. Committee culture, peer review, HR processes, social media mobs, and “best practices” often function as the rule’s unconscious war on exception. The free spirit must study these mechanisms without bitterness, recognize them operating inside himself, and refuse to let the “good people’s” astuteness define the limits of what can be thought or done. Practicing vivisection on one’s own good-natured impulses is the beginning of resistance.
Validation: Direct Kaufmann translation of Section 218. Faithful to the recommendation to study the rule’s Jesuitical struggle against the exception and the call for self-vivisection. ~305 words.
