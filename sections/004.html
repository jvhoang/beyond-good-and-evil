FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 4

Identifier: 4
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 4 delivers one of the book’s most provocative declarations: “TO RECOGNISE UNTRUTH AS A CONDITION OF LIFE.” The falseness of an opinion is not, for us, any objection to it. The decisive question is how far an opinion is life-furthering, life-preserving, species-preserving—perhaps even species-rearing. Logical fictions, the imagined world of the absolute and immutable, the constant counterfeiting of the world by means of numbers—these are not regrettable errors. Without them, Nietzsche insists, man could not live. To renounce false opinions would be to renounce life itself. A philosophy bold enough to recognize this “has thereby alone placed itself beyond good and evil.” The title of the book is therefore not a moral boast but a description of the experimental stance required once we admit that many conditions of life—including our most indispensable errors—lie outside the old moral categories.

Key Insight: Perspective, error, and fiction are not optional decorations on life; they are among its necessary conditions. The free spirit learns to affirm them consciously rather than resentfully. The renunciation of false opinions would be a renunciation of life.

Connection to the book: This is the affirmative counterpart to the Preface and Sections 1–3. Having questioned the will to truth, dismantled the prejudice of antitheses, and naturalized thought as instinctive, Nietzsche now affirms the creative, life-serving power of untruth and perspective. It marks the turn from critique to the positive project of the “philosophy of the future”: not the elimination of error, but its conscious, life-enhancing employment.

Why it matters: Science itself depends on useful fictions (models, idealizations, statistical approximations). Nations, cultures, and individuals live by necessary myths and narratives. For readers of the Kaufmann translation, the real question is no longer whether we can live without untruth, but whether we can live with it consciously, courageously, and in service of stronger, more life-affirming forms rather than self-deception or resentment. Beyond Good and Evil is itself an experiment in exactly this stance.
Companion Note: Beyond Good and Evil is itself an experiment in living with necessary fictions consciously rather than resentfully. The book models the stance it describes.
