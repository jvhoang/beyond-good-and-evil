FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 164

Sons of God Beyond Morals
Identifier: 164
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Nietzsche has Jesus say to his Jews: “The law was for servants;—love God as I love him, as his Son! What have we Sons of God to do with morals!”

The aphorism presents Jesus as an immoralist in the highest sense. The law and conventional morals are for those who require external commands—servants. For the Son who stands in direct, loving relation to God, morality in the ordinary sense is beneath or beside the point. The higher type relates immediately to the divine (or the highest value) through love and rank, not through rule-following.

This is consistent with Nietzsche’s larger portrait of Jesus in later works as a bearer of a noble, anti-legalistic spirit that was later distorted into a slave religion. Here the emphasis is on the distinction of rank: what is binding for lower types is irrelevant or even demeaning for those of higher nature.

Key Insight: Genuine nobility and proximity to the highest values render ordinary morality superfluous or inappropriate. The “Sons of God” live by a different measure.

Modern companion insight: Contemporary culture collapses all distinctions of rank into egalitarian “values.” Nietzsche reminds us that some individuals and states may legitimately stand beyond the moral code that governs the herd—not out of license, but out of a different order of soul. The free spirit asks whether our own highest aspirations are still bound by servant-morality or whether we can love and create from a higher necessity.
Key Context: Epigram reinterpreting the figure of Jesus as beyond slave morality; Part Four critique of conventional ethics.
