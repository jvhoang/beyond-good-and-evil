FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 60

Love of Mankind for God's Sake — The Noblest and Remotest Sentiment
Identifier: 60
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Context: Section 60 continues the psychological investigation of religious sentiment in Part Three of Beyond Good and Evil ("What Is Religious"). Following the analysis of superficiality and piety as fear-driven artistic falsification in Section 59, Nietzsche here isolates what has "so far been the noblest and remotest sentiment" attained by mankind: the love of mankind specifically "FOR GOD'S SAKE." This is no naive or secular humanitarianism. It requires a higher, more delicate inclination to give it proportion, salt, and refinement—otherwise it collapses into additional folly and brutishness. The section aligns with BGE's project of rank-ordering sentiments and types, preparing the instrumental use of religion by the philosopher of the future (Section 61) and the critique of pity and equality that runs throughout the book. It echoes the "pathos of distance" and the demand that even the highest moral feelings be revalued according to their service to life, strength, and the production of higher types rather than as ends in themselves. The stammering discoverer of this truth is elevated to a holy, respected figure who "has so far flown highest and gone astray in the finest fashion," underscoring Nietzsche's complex respect for rare, delicate spiritual achievements even while diagnosing their limits.

Main points/arguments: To love mankind FOR GOD'S SAKE—this has so far been the noblest and remotest sentiment to which mankind has attained. That love to mankind, without any redeeming intention in the background, is only an ADDITIONAL folly and brutishness; the inclination to this love has first to get its proportion, its delicacy, its gram of salt and sprinkling of ambergris from a higher inclination.
