FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 19

Identifier: 19 • The Will • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 19 is one of the most important and extended analyses in Part One. It directly targets the concept of "the will," which philosophers (especially Schopenhauer) had treated as the best-known and most immediately certain thing. This section is foundational for Nietzsche's positive doctrine of the will to power, which emerges as a more complex, affirmative, and multiplicitous alternative to the unified, often pessimistic "will" of earlier metaphysics. It also has profound implications for morality, as it reframes willing as a relation of command and obedience within a "social structure of many souls."

Nietzsche begins by noting the prejudice: "Philosophers are accustomed to speak of the will as though it were the best-known thing in the world; indeed, Schopenhauer has given us to understand that the will alone is really known to us, absolutely and completely known, without deduction or addition." He counters that this is another case of philosophers adopting and exaggerating a popular prejudice. Willing is "above all something COMPLICATED, something that is a unity only in name." Breaking it down: in all willing there is a plurality of sensations (the "away from which," the "towards which," and the "from" and "to" itself), plus an accompanying muscular sensation; there is always a ruling thought that cannot be severed from the willing; and, above all, the will is an emotion — "the emotion of the command." "That which is termed 'freedom of the will' is essentially the emotion of supremacy in respect to him who must obey: 'I am free, "he" must obey.'"

The strangest thing is that we are simultaneously commanding and obeying parties, yet we deceive ourselves with the synthetic term "I," leading to the false belief that willing suffices for action. "L'EFFET C'EST MOI." The body itself "is but a social structure composed of many souls." Willing-as-such belongs to the sphere of morals as "the doctrine of the relations of supremacy under which the phenomenon of 'life' manifests itself."

Key quotes:
"Willing seems to me to be above all something COMPLICATED, something that is a unity only in name..."
"the will is not only a complex of sensation and thinking, but it is above all an EMOTION, and in fact the emotion of the command."
"'Freedom of Will'—that is the expression for the complex state of delight of the person exercising volition, who commands and at the same time identifies himself with the executor of the order..."
"our body is but a social structure composed of many souls"

Philosophical implications for truth/morality/power/free spirit: The simple, unified "will" or "free will" is a fiction that conceals multiplicity, command, and the pleasure of overcoming. Morality is not about a sovereign individual's choices but about internal (and external) relations of supremacy. This directly supports the will to power as the fundamental fact: life manifests as commanding and obeying drives. For the free spirit, the task is honest acknowledgment of this complexity rather than moralistic simplification. It advances BGE by providing the psychological and physiological basis for the revaluation of values and the portrait of the noble type who affirms multiplicity and command.
Key Context: Part One of BGE. Central development of will to power as multiplicity and command; critique of Schopenhauer; foundation for later moral psychology.
