FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 49

Ancient Greek Gratitude vs. Christian Fear in Religion
Identifier: 49
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
In the broader context of Nietzsche's philosophy, Section 49 stands as a pivotal contrast within Part Three of Beyond Good and Evil ('What Is Religious'), illuminating the psychological and valuational differences between ancient Greek religiosity and the emerging Christian one. Following Section 48's discussion of religious temperaments and preceding analyses of passion for God and the saint, it exemplifies Nietzsche's recurring philhellenism—his idealization of the Greeks as exemplars of noble, life-affirming attitudes—against the backdrop of his genealogy of morals. This section echoes the Preface and early parts' attack on Christian-Platonic prejudices, positioning gratitude as the mark of a 'superior kind of man' who affirms nature and life without ressentiment, while fear signals the ascendancy of the populace and the preparation for Christianity's slave morality. In BGE's project as prelude to revaluation, it serves the psychologist's hunt by revealing religion as expression of will to power and type: the Greek as strong, generous; the Christian as fearful, vengeful preparation. It aligns with Nietzsche's earlier works like The Birth of Tragedy, where Greek tragedy and religion celebrate the Dionysian yes-saying, versus the later 'slave revolt in morality.' The main points and arguments: What is astonishing in the religious life of the ancient Greeks is the irrestrainable stream of GRATITUDE which it pours forth. It is a very superior kind of man who takes SUCH an attitude towards nature and life. Later on, when the populace got the upper hand in Greece, FEAR became rampant also in religion; and Christianity was preparing itself. The shift from gratitude to fear marks a decline in human type, from aristocratic affirmation to plebeian dread and the need for otherworldly consolation or revenge. Key quotes (Kaufmann/Zimmern style): 'That which is so astonishing in the religious life of the ancient Greeks is the irrestrainable stream of GRATITUDE which it pours forth—it is a very superior kind of man who takes SUCH an attitude towards nature and life.—Later on, when the populace got the upper hand in Greece, FEAR became rampant also in religion; and Christianity was preparing itself.' Implications for morality, truth, power, free spirit: This underscores Nietzsche's view that religious attitudes are symptoms of strength or weakness. Gratitude reflects the noble soul's overflow and yes to life (cf. will to power as creative); fear reflects the weak's need for protection and projection of hostility (ressentiment). For the free spirit and future philosopher, recovering or emulating the Greek attitude means overcoming Christian fear-based morality, enabling a more profound, affirmative religiosity or its transcendence. It critiques 'progress' narratives that favor the later developments, valuing instead the 'superior' ancient type. The section prepares for later critiques of the New Testament's pettiness (Sec 52) and the saint (Sec 51).
