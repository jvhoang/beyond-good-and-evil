FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 29

Identifier: 29 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 29 declares: "Independence is for the very few; it is a privilege of the strong." Whoever attempts it without inner constraint enters a labyrinth, multiplies dangers a thousandfold, and risks being torn piecemeal by the minotaur of conscience. Success or failure happens far from the comprehension of men; there is no going back, and no pity from men.

In BGE Part Two, this crystallizes the price of the free spirit's path after the discussions of concealment, descent, and tempo. The free spirit's solitude is not romantic but dangerous and irreversible. The labyrinth has no exit; the minotaur (conscience, self-doubt, the old morality) waits. The higher man accepts this isolation and the lack of recognition or return.

Key quotes (Kaufmann style):
"Independence is for the very few; it is a privilege of the strong."
"He enters into a labyrinth, he multiplies a thousandfold the dangers which life brings with it in any case... And he cannot go back any longer. Nor can he go back to the pity of men."
"Supposing one like that comes to grief, this happens so far from the comprehension of men that they neither feel it nor sympathize."

Implications: True free-spirited independence is not for the herd or the weak; it is a form of power that isolates and endangers. This advances the book's critique by rejecting egalitarian notions of "freedom" or independence; the free spirit is the strong exception who accepts the minotaur and the lack of return or pity.

Key Takeaway: Genuine independence is rare, costly, and irreversible. It is not a right or a comfortable choice but a privilege and a danger reserved for the strong who can endure the labyrinth without pity or return.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Builds directly on the concealment (25), immersion (26), difficulty (27), and tempo (28) of the free spirit. Prepares the portrait of the philosopher of the future who experiments at great personal risk. The labyrinth and minotaur imagery recur in Nietzsche’s later work as symbols of the cost of revaluation.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Read the long sentence describing the labyrinth slowly. The “minotaur of conscience” is especially powerful—the old morality, internalized, becomes the most dangerous enemy of the independent one. No going back is both tragedy and liberation.

Why it matters today: In a culture that celebrates “independence,” “authenticity,” and “speaking your truth” as easy, empowering choices available to everyone, Nietzsche’s warning is sobering. Real intellectual, moral, or creative independence often means professional isolation, misunderstanding, loss of community, and no guarantee of success or recognition. It is not for the many who seek safety in numbers or validation. The strong pay the price gladly because the alternative (conformity) is worse.
Companion Polish: Independence is not a pose or a lifestyle brand. It is a labyrinth with a minotaur. Only the strong enter knowingly, and even they may be torn. The free spirit chooses this fate because remaining in the herd is a slower death of the spirit.
