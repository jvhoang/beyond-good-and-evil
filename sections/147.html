FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 147

The Stick for Good and Bad Women
Identifier: 147
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 147 states: “From old Florentine novels—moreover, from life: Buona femmina e mala femmina vuol bastone.—Sacchetti, Nov. 86.”

Nietzsche cites an old Italian proverb: both the good woman and the bad woman want the stick. The “bastone” (stick, rod, or staff) can be read as discipline, authority, physical correction, or the phallic symbol of male power. The saying collapses the moral distinction between “good” and “bad” women in their underlying desire or need for firm handling. It is presented as drawn from both literary tradition and observed life.

The epigram is deliberately crude and unromantic. It refuses the Victorian or Christian idealization of the good woman as naturally obedient and gentle. Instead, it suggests a common underlying psychology or erotic economy: women of both moral types respond to, or even require, the exercise of force or authority. This is not an endorsement of brutality but a register of a certain realism about power relations between the sexes as Nietzsche observed them in history and literature.

Key Insight: Moral categories of women may mask a shared desire or responsiveness to authoritative power expressed as the “stick.”

Connection to BGE themes: Part Four contains several unsparing remarks on women and power. This continues the project of seeing sexual relations without the veil of idealization. It pairs with remarks on love as barbarity, adornment, and revenge. The free spirit accepts such observations as data for a naturalistic psychology rather than as invitations to moral outrage or sentiment.

Modern companion insight: In 2026, with renewed cultural interest in traditional gender roles, “tradwife” aesthetics, BDSM normalization, and debates over domestic discipline or “soft patriarchy,” the proverb resurfaces in disguised forms. Progressive discourse treats any suggestion of female desire for authority or correction as toxic or abusive. The free spirit examines the actual lived dynamics in relationships and history without ideological filters. Whether the “stick” is literal, symbolic, or erotic, the observation points to persistent patterns of power and desire that slogans of equality do not erase. One studies the reality rather than the wished-for narrative.
Key Context: Citation of Italian proverb on women’s responsiveness to authority. Part Four realist observations on sex and power.
