FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 21

Identifier: 21
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
The CAUSA SUI is the best self-contradiction yet conceived—a logical violation and unnaturalness. Yet the extravagant pride of man has entangled itself profoundly with this folly. The desire for “freedom of will” in the superlative, metaphysical sense (still holding sway in the minds of the half-educated) is the desire to bear the entire and ultimate responsibility for one’s actions oneself and to absolve God, the world, ancestors, chance, and society. It involves nothing less than being precisely this CAUSA SUI and, with more than Munchausen daring, pulling oneself up into existence by the hair out of the slough of nothingness.

Breakdown of the argument:
• Nietzsche urges carrying “enlightenment” a step further: once the crass stupidity of “free will” is put out of one’s head, also discard its contrary—the “non-free will,” which is a misuse of cause and effect.
• One should not wrongly materialise “cause” and “effect” as natural philosophers do (mechanical doltishness). Use them only as pure conceptions, conventional fictions for designation and mutual understanding—not for explanation.
• In “being-in-itself” there is nothing of causal-connection, necessity, or psychological non-freedom. It is WE alone who have devised cause, sequence, reciprocity, law, freedom, motive, and purpose. When we interpret this symbol-world as “being-in-itself,” we act mythologically once more.

Key Takeaway: The entire debate of free will vs. determinism is a grammatical and psychological fiction. There is no first cause or self-caused being; everything belongs to the chain or, more deeply, to the will to power. Moral responsibility built on the fiction of the causa sui collapses.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Directly follows the critique of the “I” (20) and atomism. Continues the exposure of metaphysical fictions that prop up traditional morality. Prepares the free spirit who can live without these consoling lies and the revaluation that follows.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Pay special attention to the Munchausen image and the warning against materialising cause/effect. Nietzsche is not denying regularity in experience; he is denying that our concepts of cause and freedom map onto an ultimate “in-itself.”

Why it matters today: Neuroscience, behavioral economics, and compatibilist debates still wrestle with these fictions. Legal and moral systems continue to rest on the myth of the ultimate self-caused responsible agent. Nietzsche’s move liberates us to ask more honest questions about power, drives, and how we might affirm life without the old metaphysical scaffolding.
Companion Polish: This section is a scalpel for cutting through one of philosophy’s most persistent knots. The free spirit does not become a fatalist; rather, the disappearance of the causa sui opens space for a richer, more affirmative responsibility grounded in the will to power.
