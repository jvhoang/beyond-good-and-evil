FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 80

Self-Respect in Self-Despising
Identifier: 80
Simple modern summary

Even when you hate yourself, you still respect yourself as the person doing the hating. Self-contempt secretly keeps you in a superior judge role. Online self-critique often performs humility while boosting status.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Epigram)
Context

Even when a person despises himself, he still respects the part of himself that is capable of that self-contempt.

Key Insights

The epigram states: "Whoever despises himself still respects himself as one who despises." The core paradox: self-contempt is never a pure negation or annihilation of the self. The very act of despising installs the despiser in a position of superiority or insight. there remains a kernel of self-respect, a ranking in which the one who passes judgment claims a higher vantage. This is will to power operating even in apparent self-abasement. The despiser preserves a subtle pride in his own acuity or honesty.

Connection to the book

In BGE this connects to the critique of self-denial ideals and morality of the weak (Part Three and elsewhere): what presents as humility or selflessness often conceals an inverted will to power. The noble soul, by contrast, practices self-reverence even while acknowledging its own limitations and the necessity of self-overcoming. Self-contempt that remains only contempt is sterile or vengeful. when it retains the respect of the one who sees clearly, it can become a tool for growth rather than self-laceration. For the rethinking of values: The free spirit and future philosopher must cultivate intellectual conscience that includes ruthless self-appraisal without collapsing into the self-undermining of the weak. Genuine rank-awareness begins with this honest double vision toward oneself.

Modern Companion Insight

Today: today's culture is saturated with public self-criticism, performative "accountability," imposter syndrome personal admission, and social-media self-deprecation that paradoxically elevates the speaker's moral or intellectual status. Nietzsche's epigram asks us to notice the respect hidden inside the despising — and to ask whether our self-critiques serve life and higher types or merely a new, subtle tyranny of the weak over the strong (including over ourselves).
