FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 80

Self-Respect in Self-Despising
Identifier: 80
Simple modern summary

Even when you hate yourself, you still respect yourself as the person doing the hating. Self-contempt secretly keeps you in a superior judge role. Online self-critique often performs humility while boosting status.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Epigram)
In the context of Nietzsche's Beyond Good and Evil, Section 80 belongs to the concise, penetrating Epigrams and Interludes of Part Four. These aphorisms model the psychological acuity and stylistic economy of the free spirit after the preparatory work of the first three parts.

The epigram states: "Whoever despises himself still respects himself as one who despises." Its core insight is a paradox of self-relation: self-contempt is never a pure negation or annihilation of the self. The very act of despising installs the despiser in a position of superiority or insight; there remains a kernel of self-respect, a ranking in which the one who passes judgment claims a higher vantage. This is will to power operating even in apparent self-abasement. The despiser preserves a subtle pride in his own acuity or honesty.

Within BGE this resonates with the critique of ascetic ideals and slave morality (Part Three and elsewhere): what presents as humility or selflessness often conceals an inverted will to power. The noble soul, by contrast, practices self-reverence even while acknowledging its own limitations and the necessity of self-overcoming. Self-contempt that remains only contempt is sterile or vengeful; when it retains the respect of the one who sees clearly, it can become a tool for growth rather than self-laceration.

Implications for the revaluation: The free spirit and future philosopher must cultivate intellectual conscience that includes ruthless self-appraisal without collapsing into the self-undermining of the weak. Genuine rank-consciousness begins with this honest double vision toward oneself.

Modern relevance: Contemporary culture is saturated with public self-criticism, performative "accountability," imposter syndrome confessions, and social-media self-deprecation that paradoxically elevates the speaker's moral or intellectual status. Nietzsche's epigram asks us to notice the respect hidden inside the despising — and to ask whether our self-critiques serve life and higher types or merely a new, subtle tyranny of the weak over the strong (including over ourselves).
