FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 257

Elevation of the Type "Man" Through Aristocratic Society
Identifier: 257
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Part Nine opens with the fundamental historical claim: "EVERY elevation of the type 'man,' has hitherto been the work of an aristocratic society and so it will always be — a society believing in a long scale of gradations of rank and differences of worth among human beings, and requiring slavery in some form or other."

Without the PATHOS OF DISTANCE — the constant out-looking and down-looking of the ruling caste on subordinates, the practice of obeying and commanding, keeping down and keeping at a distance — that more mysterious pathos could never have arisen: the longing for an ever new widening of distance within the soul itself, the formation of ever higher, rarer, further, more extended, more comprehensive states — in short, the elevation of the type "man," the continued "self-surmounting of man."

One must not harbor humanitarian illusions about the origin of aristocratic society. The truth is hard. Every higher civilization has originated when men with a still natural nature — barbarians in every terrible sense, men of prey still in possession of unbroken strength of will and desire for power — threw themselves upon weaker, more moral, more peaceful races or upon old mellow civilizations in which the final vital force was flickering out. At the commencement, the noble caste was always the barbarian caste; their superiority lay in their psychical power — they were more COMPLETE men (which at every point also implies the same as "more complete beasts").

Key Insight: The pathos of distance is the precondition for the internal self-overcoming that produces higher humanity. Rank, slavery (in some form), and the hard reality of conquest are not regrettable accidents but the historical means of the elevation of the type man.

Modern companion insight: Egalitarian ideologies that deny all rank and distance risk flattening the very conditions for greatness and self-surmounting. The free spirit studies the genealogy of nobility without moralistic softening.
Validation: Drawn directly from the opening of Section 257. Faithful to the aristocratic origin of all elevation, the pathos of distance as inner and outer precondition, the barbarian caste as historical noble type, and the rejection of humanitarian illusions. ~340 words.
