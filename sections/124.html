FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 124

Exulting at the Stake
Identifier: 124
Simple modern summary

The person celebrating at the stake often is not conquering pain—they are relieved it hurts less than expected. Triumph over suffering can be triumph over fear of pain. Heroism sometimes means the agony never arrived as imagined.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

People often prefer comforting illusions and certainty over uncomfortable truths, because those illusions help us live a certain kind of life.

Key insight

The martyr or hero who appears to conquer suffering through strength is often not enduring more pain than expected but discovering that the anticipated agony has not materialized — or has been transformed by expectation, adrenaline, faith, or pride into something bearable or even ecstatic. The triumph is not over pain itself but over the fear of a pain that failed to arrive in the expected form. The parable generalizes to many human “victories.” Much apparent heroism and stoicism consists in the relief or surprise that reality was less terrible than the imagination had prepared us for. Expectation often creates the greater suffering.

BGE connection

This fits Nietzsche’s interest in the psychology of the ascetic, the saint, and the “higher” types who appear to transcend ordinary human limits. It undermines romanticized views of suffering as inherently ennobling while pointing to the power of interpretation and expectation (perspectivism). The free spirit examines whether proclaimed victories over pain or fate rest on actual strength or on miscalculation and self-deception. rethinking of values includes refusing to worship suffering for its own sake.

Modern companion

In 2026, with widespread anxiety, catastrophic thinking amplified by news and social media, and performative resilience on display, this parable is highly applicable. Many people “exult” after surviving anticipated disasters (job loss, breakup, public failure, health scares) not because they heroically endured extreme pain, but because the experience was far less devastating than their catastrophic expectations. Mental health practices that help recalibrate expectations and reduce anticipatory dread are practicing a version of this insight. The free spirit cultivates accurate assessment of likely suffering rather than inflating it for dramatic self-narrative, and learns to recognize when relief masquerades as triumph. Real strength includes not needing to dramatize one’s endurance. The parable cautions against both masochistic glorification of pain and inflated claims of having conquered it.

Key Context: Epigrammatic parable on expectation, suffering, and the psychology of apparent heroism in Part Four. Deepens the analysis of self-deception and the value of accurate perspective.
