FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 265

Egoism as the Essence of the Noble Soul
Identifier: 265
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
"At the risk of displeasing innocent ears, I submit that egoism belongs to the essence of a noble soul, I mean the unalterable belief that to a being such as 'we,' other beings must naturally be in subjection, and have to sacrifice themselves."

The noble soul accepts this egoism without question or consciousness of harshness, as something that may have its basis in the primary law of things: "It is justice itself." Under certain circumstances he acknowledges other equally privileged ones; among equals he moves with assurance of modesty and delicate respect, honoring himself in them. The exchange of honours and rights is the essence of all intercourse among peers.

The notion of "favour" has neither significance nor good repute among equals. He looks "aloft" unwillingly — he looks forward, horizontally and deliberately, or downwards. HE KNOWS THAT HE IS ON A HEIGHT.

Key Insight: Noble egoism is not petty selfishness but the natural self-reverence and order-of-rank consciousness of the higher type. It is just that the exceptional stand above and that others may serve the higher.

Modern companion insight: Modern culture often equates any self-assertion or rank-consciousness with vice. Nietzsche restores the legitimacy of the noble soul's natural perspective: one is on a height and knows it without apology.
Validation: Direct from text. Captures the unapologetic egoism, justice among unequals, peer intercourse, and the knowledge of height. ~280 words.
