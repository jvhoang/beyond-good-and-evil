FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART EIGHT

Section 255

Precautions Against German Music: Toward a Super-European Art
Identifier: 255
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Section 255 warns the Southerner: “I hold that many precautions should be taken against German music... Such a Southerner, a Southerner not by origin but by BELIEF, if he should dream of the future of music, must also dream of it being freed from the influence of the North; and must have in his ears the prelude to a deeper, mightier, and perhaps more perverse and mysterious music, a super-German music... a super-European music, which holds its own even in presence of the brown sunsets of the desert, whose soul is akin to the palm-tree...”

German music injures taste and health; it fades before the Mediterranean. The desired art knows nothing more of good and evil, only occasional sailor’s homesickness and golden shadows.

Key Insight: The future of higher art lies beyond the North’s fog—deeper, more sensuous, more affirmative, beyond moralism.

BGE connection: Part Eight’s national diagnosis turns affirmative: the good European must overcome German (and English) limitations toward a new synthesis that includes the South without sentimentality.

Modern companion insight: Much contemporary “serious” music and art remains heavy, conceptual, or moralizing in a Northern Protestant mode. The free spirit listens for and cultivates the super-European—art that can stand under the desert sun, at home with beautiful dangerous beasts, beyond the categories of good and evil. This is not escapism but the creation of a taste capable of the future.
Validation: Direct Kaufmann of Section 255. Faithful to the warning against German music and the vision of super-European music beyond good and evil. ~285 words.
