FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 25

Identifier: 25 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
After the cheerful commencement of Section 24 on the necessary simplifications and falsifications that make life and knowledge possible, Section 25 issues a serious warning to philosophers and friends of knowledge: beware of martyrdom! Of suffering “for the truth’s sake”! Even of defending yourselves! It spoils all the innocence and fine neutrality of your conscience; makes you headstrong against objections and red rags; it stupefies, animalizes, and brutalizes. In the struggle with danger, slander, suspicion, expulsion, and worse, you pose as protectors of truth upon earth—as though “the truth” were such an innocuous and incompetent creature as to require protectors! And you of all people, you knights of the most sorrowful countenances, dear loafers and cobweb-spinners of the spirit!

Breakdown of the argument:
• You know well enough that it cannot be of any consequence if you of all people are proved right; you know that no philosopher so far has been proved right. There might be a more laudable truthfulness in every little question mark that you place after your special words and favorite doctrines (and occasionally after yourselves) than in all the solemn gestures and trumps before accusers and law courts.
• Rather, go away. Flee into concealment. And have your masks and subtlety, that you may be mistaken for what you are not, or feared a little. And don’t forget the garden, the garden with golden trelliswork. And have around you who are as a garden—or as music on the waters in the evening, when the day is turning into memories.
• Choose the solitude, the free, playful, light solitude that gives you, too, the right to remain good in some sense. How poisonous, how crafty, how bad, does every long war make one that cannot be waged openly by means of force! How personal does a long fear make one, long watching of enemies, of possible enemies! These outcasts of society, these long-pursued, wickedly persecuted ones—also the compulsory recluses, the Spinozas or Giordano Brunos—always become in the end, even under the most spiritual masquerade, and perhaps without being themselves aware of it, sophisticated vengeance-seekers and poison-brewers.

Key Takeaway: The true philosopher of the future protects his independence and cheerfulness by strategic concealment, masks, and playful solitude rather than by heroic public defense of “the truth.” Martyrdom corrupts the spirit. The free spirit cultivates a garden-like inwardness and the right to remain “good” in a higher, non-moral sense.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Directly follows the affirmation of necessary falsification (24) with practical counsel for living it. Prepares the portrait of the free spirit who experiments, hides, and creates without becoming a new dogmatist or victim. Echoes the Preface’s call for very free spirits who sustain tension without public clamor.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Note the tonal shift from the joyful “O sancta simplicitas” to this grave, almost conspiratorial advice. The images of the golden-trellis garden and evening music on water are deliberate antidotes to the poison of persecution. The Spinozas and Brunos reference is both warning and honor.

Why it matters today: In an age of online pile-ons, cancel culture, institutional loyalty tests, and the professionalization of outrage, Nietzsche’s counsel is urgent: the serious seeker of knowledge must sometimes choose strategic invisibility and private gardens over public heroism. Independence is preserved not by winning arguments in the agora but by refusing to be drawn into its corrupting dynamics.
Companion Polish: This section is practical wisdom for the free spirit. Martyrdom is not proof of truth but of a failure of subtlety and self-protection. The philosopher’s real work happens in concealment, with lightness.
HIGHLIGHTED QUOTES
"Take care, philosophers and friends, of knowledge, and beware of martyrdom! Of suffering 'for the truth's sake'! Even of defending yourselves!"
"Rather, go away. Flee into concealment. And have your masks and subtlety, that you may be mistaken for what you are not, or feared a little."
"The martyrdom of the philosopher, his 'sacrifice for the sake of truth,' forces into the light whatever of the agitator and actor lurks in him... merely the continued proof that the long, real tragedy is at an end, assuming that every philosophy was in its genesis a long tragedy."
