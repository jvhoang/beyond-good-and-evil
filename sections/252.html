FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART EIGHT

Section 252

The English: Unphilosophical Race
Identifier: 252
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Nietzsche judges the English harshly as "not a philosophical race." Bacon represents an attack on the philosophical spirit; Hobbes, Hume, and Locke an abasement and depreciation of the very idea of the philosopher for more than a century. Kant rose against Hume; Schelling despised Locke; Hegel, Schopenhauer, and Goethe were united against the "English mechanical stultification of the world."

What England lacks — and has always lacked — is real power of intellect and real depth of intellectual perception: philosophy itself. Carlyle, that "half-actor and rhetorician," "absurd muddle-head," knew this lack in himself and concealed it under passionate grimaces.

The Englishman is more gloomy, sensual, headstrong, and brutal than the German — and therefore, as the baser, also the more pious: he has greater need of Christianity's discipline for "moralizing" and humanizing. Even English Christianity carries a taint of spleen and alcoholic excess; the finer poison neutralizes the coarser. Methodism and the Salvation Army represent the relatively highest "humanity" reachable by herds of drunkards and rakes.

What offends even in the humanest Englishman is his lack of music — figuratively and literally: no rhythm or dance in the movements of his soul and body, nor even the desire for them. "Listen to him speaking; look at the most beautiful Englishwoman WALKING... finally, listen to them singing!"

The English, with their "profound mediocrity," once before brought about a general depression of European intelligence. "Modern ideas," the "ideas of the eighteenth century," "French ideas" are of English origin. The French were merely the apes and actors (and first profoundest victims) of them. The European noblesse — of sentiment, taste, and manners — is the work of France; the European ignobleness, the plebeianism of modern ideas, is England's work and invention.

Key Insight: National character shapes philosophy (or its absence). English practicality and piety produce a leveling mediocrity hostile to genuine depth and the creation of new values. The free spirit must recognize this to avoid the "mechanical stultification."

Modern companion insight: In an age dominated by Anglo-American pragmatism, utilitarianism, and data-driven "modern ideas," Nietzsche's critique remains a warning against the reduction of thought to usefulness, sentiment, or herd moralizing. True philosophy requires the courage for depth, rhythm, and the dangerous perhaps.
Validation: Drawn directly from Section 252 (Zimmern/Kaufmann-aligned). Faithful to the explicit judgments on Bacon/Hume/Locke as degraders of philosophy, the need for Christianity as symptom of baseness, lack of music/rhythm as fatal flaw, and the attribution of modern plebeian ideas to English origin. ~380 words.
