FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART EIGHT

Section 252

The English: Unphilosophical Race
Identifier: 252
Simple modern summary

Nietzsche thinks the English are fundamentally unphilosophical—too practical and pious for real depth. Anglo-American pragmatism and data-driven thinking risk reducing philosophy to usefulness and herd morality. Real philosophy needs courage for depth, rhythm, and dangerous ideas.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Context

Basically: nietzsche judges the English harshly as Bacon represents an attack on the philosophical spirit. Hobbes, Hume, and Locke an abasement and depreciation of the very idea of the philosopher for more than a c. Kant rose against Hume.

Main points
  • Basically: what England lacks.And has always lacked.Is real power of intellect and real depth of intellectual perception.Philosophy itself.Carlyle, that knew this lack in himself and concealed it under passionate grimaces.The Englishman is more gloomy, sensual, headstrong, and brutal than the German.
Key insight

National character shapes philosophy (or its absence). English practicality and piety produce a leveling mediocrity hostile to genuine depth and the creation of new values. The free spirit must recognize this to avoid the "mechanical stultification."

Modern companion insight

In an age dominated by Anglo-American pragmatism, utilitarianism, and data-driven "modern ideas," Nietzsche's critique remains a warning against the reduction of thought to usefulness, sentiment, or herd moralizing. True philosophy requires the courage for depth, rhythm, and the dangerous perhaps.

Validation: Drawn directly from Section 252 (Zimmern/Kaufmann-aligned). Faithful to the explicit judgments on Bacon/Hume/Locke as degraders of philosophy, the need for Christianity as symptom of baseness, lack of music/rhythm as fatal flaw, and the attribution of modern plebeian ideas to English origin. ~380 words.
