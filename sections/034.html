FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 34

Identifier: 34 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Whatever philosophical standpoint one may adopt today, from every point of view the erroneousness of the world in which we think we live is the surest and firmest fact that we can lay eyes on: we find reasons upon reasons for it which would like to lure us to hypotheses concerning a deceptive principle in “the essence of things.” But whoever holds our thinking itself, “the spirit,” in other words, responsible for the falseness of the world — an honorable way out which is chosen by every conscious or unconscious advocatus dei — whoever takes this world, along with space, time, form, movement, to be falsely inferred — anyone like that would at least have ample reason to learn to be suspicious at long last of all thinking. Wouldn't thinking have put over on us the biggest hoax yet? And what warrant would there be that it would not continue to do what it has always done? In all seriousness: the innocence of our thinkers is somehow touching and evokes reverence, when today they still step before consciousness with the request that it should please give them honest answers; for example, whether it is “real,” and why it so resolutely keeps the external world at a distance, and other questions of that kind. The faith in “immediate certainties” is a moral naïveté that reflects honor on us philosophers; but — after all we should not be “merely moral” men. Apart from morality, this faith is a stupidity that reflects little honor on us. In bourgeois life ever-present suspicion may be called a sign of “bad character”; on this point we philosophers must go further and say: the faith in immediate certainties is a stupidity that reflects little honor on us.

Key Takeaway: The world we live in is erroneous/falsified at the most fundamental level (space, time, cause, self, etc.). To blame “the spirit” or thinking itself for this falseness is a tempting but still moral/naive move. The free spirit learns radical suspicion even of thinking and “immediate certainties.” Faith in consciousness as truthful is a stupidity once one steps beyond morality.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This is the epistemological and metaphysical culmination of Part One’s critique of prejudices and the opening of Part Two. It radicalizes the insight of Section 24 (necessary falsification) and prepares the will to power as the deeper “text” behind our interpretations. The attack on “immediate certainties” and the “innocence of thinkers” is central to Nietzsche’s naturalism and perspectivism.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The section is dense and ironic. Note the move from “erroneousness of the world” to suspicion of thinking itself, and the final jab at “bourgeois” moral suspicion vs. philosophical radical suspicion. The “advocatus dei” is a key target.

Why it matters today: In cognitive science, neuroscience, and AI, we rediscover that our “immediate” experience of self, objects, time, and causality is a constructed model full of useful fictions. Nietzsche’s call for suspicion of thinking itself (not just of this or that belief) anticipates the hard problem of consciousness and the limits of introspection. It also critiques the residual moral innocence in much “critical thinking” education that still trusts consciousness once biases are “debunked.” The free spirit goes further: even the debunker must be suspected.
Companion Polish: The erroneousness is not a bug to be fixed by better thinking; it is the condition of life. The free spirit does not seek “true” immediate certainties but learns to navigate and affirm the necessary falsifications with eyes open.
