FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 11

Identifier: 11 • Kant, Faculties and the German Dream • Part One
Simple modern summary

Kant and German philosophers answered hard questions by inventing mysterious faculties—which just restated the question in fancier words. It is like saying a sleeping pill works because it has a sleeping virtue. Ask why we need to believe something, not just how it is possible.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 11 delivers one of Nietzsche's sharpest satirical attacks on post-Kantian German philosophy. Kant was proudest of his Categories and of having discovered a "new faculty in man." His answer to "How are synthetic judgments a priori POSSIBLE?" was, in essence, "BY MEANS OF A MEANS (faculty)"—a circumlocution so imposingly German that the "comical niaiserie allemande" was lost on the audience. The jubilation peaked when Kant "further discovered a moral faculty." Then came "the honeymoon of German philosophy." Young theologians sought "faculties" in the groves; Schelling named one "intellectual intuition," gratifying the "naturally pious-inclined Germans."

It was "really youthfulness, notwithstanding that it disguised itself so boldly, in hoary and senile conceptions." Kant's "by means of a means" was no answer—merely a repetition of the question, like Molière's doctor explaining opium's power by its "virtus dormitiva." The proper question is not "How are synthetic judgments a priori possible?" but "Why is belief in such judgments necessary?"—for the preservation of creatures like ourselves. They "still might naturally be false judgments!" "German philosophy" exercised enormous influence as a "VIRTUS DORMITIVA," an "antidote to the still overwhelming sensualism" of the previous century—"sensus assoupire."

Key Insight: Post-Kantian "faculty"-hunting and "intellectual intuition" were not genuine answers but repetitions of the question dressed in impressive language—a soporific that lulled critical faculties and preserved moral/religious comforts against Enlightenment sensualism. Belief in synthetic a priori judgments may be necessary for life even if the judgments themselves are false.

Connection to the book: This sharpens the critique of "prejudices of philosophers" (Preface through Section 10). It exposes how philosophy can function as cultural anesthetic and power-preserving fiction, preparing the free spirit's demand for honesty about the life-conditions and power-interests served by claims. It links to the suspicion of "immediate certainties" (Section 16) and the call for a more rigorous, experimental psychology.

Why it matters: Contemporary discourse is full of "by means of a means" black-box explanations ("emergent properties," "modules," "intuitions") invoked to explain without explaining. The demand to ask "Why do we need to believe this?" rather than "How is it possible?" remains a powerful tool against ideology, pseudoscience, and systems that lull critical thought to preserve comfort or power. It cautions against mistaking ornate or "profound" language for genuine insight.
Companion Note: "By means of a means"—the perfect image of philosophical evasion. Use the slides to enjoy the satire; then read the Kaufmann text alert to every modern "faculty" or black box that repeats the question while sounding like an answer.
HIGHLIGHTED QUOTES
"Kant was first and foremost proud of his Table of Categories; with it in his hand he said: 'This is the most difficult thing that could ever be undertaken on behalf of metaphysics.'"
"'By means of a means (faculty)'—he had said, or at least meant to say. But, is that—an answer? An explanation? Or is it not rather merely a repetition of the question?"
"synthetic judgments a priori should not 'be possible' at all; we have no right to them; in our mouths they are nothing but false judgments. Only, of course, the belief in their truth is necessary..."
