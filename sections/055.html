FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 55

The Ladder of Religious Cruelty and Sacrifice of God
Identifier: 55
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
In the broader context of Nietzsche's philosophy, Section 55 stands as a central aphorism in Part Three ("What Is Religious") of Beyond Good and Evil. It encapsulates the genealogical method applied to religion, tracing a "ladder of religious cruelty" that progresses through history and culminates in the modern sacrifice of God himself for nothingness. This builds on the critique of the soul in Section 54 and prepares the positive counter-ideal of eternal recurrence in Section 56. It exemplifies BGE's project of diagnosing the religious nature as a phenomenon of self-torment and will to power, advancing the revaluation of values by exposing how morality and piety have been forms of cruelty turned inward against life itself. Following his earlier works, Nietzsche treats religion not as error alone but as a profound, if ultimately nihilistic, expression of human drives.

The main points and arguments: There is a great ladder of religious cruelty, with many rounds; but three of these are the most important. Once on a time men sacrificed human beings to their God, and perhaps just those they loved the best—to this category belong the firstling sacrifices of all primitive religions, and also the sacrifice of the Emperor Tiberius in the Mithra-Grotto on the Island of Capri, that most terrible of all Roman anachronisms. Then, during the moral epoch of mankind, they sacrificed to their God the strongest instincts they possessed, their "nature"; THIS festal joy shines in the cruel glances of ascetics and "anti-natural" fanatics. Finally, what still remained to be sacrificed? Was it not necessary in the end for men to sacrifice everything comforting, holy, healing, all hope, all faith in hidden harmonies, in future blessedness and justice? Was it not necessary to sacrifice God himself, and out of cruelty to themselves to worship stone, stupidity, gravity, fate, nothingness? To sacrifice God for nothingness—this paradoxical mystery of the ultimate cruelty has been reserved for the rising generation; we all know something thereof already.

Key quotes from the Kaufmann translation: "There is a great ladder of religious cruelty, with many rounds; but three of these are the most important." "Then, during the moral epoch of mankind, they sacrificed to their God the strongest instincts they possessed, their 'nature'; THIS festal joy shines in the cruel glances of ascetics and 'anti-natural' fanatics." "To sacrifice God for nothingness—this paradoxical mystery of the ultimate cruelty has been reserved for the rising generation; we all know something thereof already."

Implications for morality, truth, power, free spirit: The history of religion is revealed as a history of increasing self-cruelty against life, moving from external sacrifice to the internal destruction of instincts and finally to the destruction of God and all transcendent meaning. This culminates in nihilism, yet the religious instinct or craving persists as the worship of nothingness. Traditional morality, rooted in such cruelty, must be overcome. The "rising generation" (free spirits and new philosophers) knows this mystery and is positioned to move beyond the ladder toward life-affirmation, amor fati, and the revaluation of all values. Power dynamics are exposed: the weak or resentful turn cruelty upon themselves and the world in the name of piety. For the free spirit, this diagnosis clears the ground for a new, exuberant spirituality that affirms rather than denies.
