Section 208: Scepticism and the Paralysis of the Will

Part Six: We Scholars • Beyond Good and Evil

Simple modern summary

Modern scepticism often comes from a sick inability to act or commit, not from real strength. Doubt becomes a sedative that avoids saying yes or no with force. A living philosophy needs will, not endless hesitation.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

Basically: section 208 diagnoses modern European scepticism as the spiritual expression of a profound bodily and cultu. paralysis of the will. When a philosopher today declares he is not a sceptic, timid contemporaries hear spiritual dynamite.

Key Kaufmann quotes
  • “The time for small politics is over. The next century is already bringing on the battle for the mastery of the earth—the compulsion to grand politics.”
Main argument
  • Basically: scepticism arises from the sudden, radical mixing of classes and races.The new generation inherits conflicting standards.Everything becomes restlessness, doubt, experiment.The will itself is what degenerates most.People lose the bold pleasure in willing and even doubt in their dreams.
Modern companion insight

Our own era of rapid globalization, mass migration, media saturation, and institutional fragmentation reproduces the conditions Nietzsche described. The triumph of “critical thinking” without commitment, endless “both-sides” discourse, and institutional risk-aversion often mask a deeper inability to will decisively. Objective scholarship and scientific neutrality can become elegant disguises for the same paralysis. The free spirit recognizes that Europe (and the West) may still require either an inner revolution that forges a long, fearful, collective will capable of setting goals for centuries — or the external pressure of a greater danger that finally forces it to acquire one. Small politics, academic hedging, and polite scepticism will not suffice for the tasks ahead.
