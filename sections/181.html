FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 181

It Is Inhuman to Bless When Cursed
Identifier: 181
Simple modern summary

Blessing someone while they are cursing you is not saintly—it is inhuman and fake in Nietzsche's view. Real responses should match real conflict. Forced sweetness can be another kind of lie.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
“It is inhuman to bless when one is being cursed.”

The demand to “bless them that curse you” is superhuman or subhuman—inhuman in the precise sense. Under active attack or insult, the natural and honest response is not to return blessing. To force blessing in that moment is either hypocrisy or an unnatural suppression that weakens the self. Nietzsche rejects the Christian inversion as a denial of healthy affect.

The epigram defends the right to appropriate response: curse for curse, or at least honest non-blessing. The free spirit does not pretend to a saintly transcendence he does not feel.

Key Insight: Moral ideals that require one to negate natural reactions under duress are often cruel to the one who tries to live them.

BGE connection: Continues the critique of Christian and modern moral “virtues” that demand the impossible or the self-destructive.

Modern companion insight: “Kill them with kindness” or mandatory positivity in the face of hostility is often a form of self-betrayal. The free spirit can choose strategic response without pretending that blessing the curser is the only noble option.
Validation: Kaufmann-aligned epigram. ~265 words.
