FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 10

Identifier: 10 • The Real and the Apparent World • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 10 diagnoses a peculiarly contemporary European obsession: the eager, subtle, even crafty handling of the ancient problem of "the real and the apparent world." "The eagerness and subtlety, I should even say craftiness, with which the problem of 'the real and the apparent world' is dealt with at present throughout Europe, furnishes food for thought and attention; and he who hears only a 'Will to Truth' in the background, and nothing else, cannot certainly boast of the sharpest ears."

In rare cases it may be genuine "metaphysician's ambition of the forlorn hope" seeking "a handful of 'certainty'" over possibilities. More often it is the "puritanical fanatics of conscience" who "prefer to put their last trust in a sure nothing, rather than in an uncertain something"—nihilism, "the sign of a despairing, mortally wearied soul." Stronger, livelier thinkers "eager for life" side "AGAINST appearance," speaking superciliously of "perspective" and devaluing their own bodies like old "ocular evidence that 'the earth stands still.'" They may be trying to recover "the 'immortal soul,' perhaps 'the old God'"—ideas by which one could "live better, that is to say, more vigorously and more joyously, than by 'modern ideas.'"

There is "DISTRUST of these modern ideas," "satiety and scorn" at the "BRIC-A-BRAC of ideas" and the "village-fair motleyness and patchiness" of reality-philosophasters. Nietzsche finds common ground with the "skeptical anti-realists": their instinct "which repels them from MODERN reality, is unrefuted." "A little MORE strength, swing, courage, and artistic power, and they would be 'OFF—and not back!'"

Key Insight: The flight from the "apparent" world is rarely pure will to truth. It is often either weary nihilism (preferring a "sure nothing") or a disguised attempt to recover old faiths for more vigorous life—or simply the healthy instinct to get "AWAY" from patchwork modern "reality." The "apparent" world is the only one; strength lies in artistic engagement with it, not escape.

Connection to the book: This extends the attack on metaphysical dualism (real/apparent, soul/body) that underpins traditional morality (see Preface, Sections 2, 4). It deepens the suspicion of "modern ideas" and prepares perspectivism and will to power: the free spirit affirms and shapes the only world there is rather than fleeing into certainty, nothingness, or nostalgic recovery. It clears space for life-affirming interpretation over both crude positivism and exhausted metaphysics.

Why it matters: Contemporary culture is awash in curated "bric-a-brac" realities (social media, virtual worlds, competing narratives, scientistic popularizations). Nietzsche's diagnostic warns against both nihilistic despair and reactionary flight to old certainties. For readers of the Kaufmann translation, it calls for greater "artistic power" and courage to engage appearance as the arena of life and creation—rather than fleeing it. Strength lies in remaining within and shaping the world we actually inhabit.
Companion Note: Part One of BGE. The "apparent" world is not to be escaped or reduced; it is the arena of creative power. Use the slides to contrast the weary flight from appearance with the affirmative leap "OFF—not back!" Pair with Kaufmann to sharpen the eye for modern forms of the same temptation.
