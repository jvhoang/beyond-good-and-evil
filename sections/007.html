FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 7

Identifier: 7
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
“How malicious philosophers can be! I know of nothing more stinging than the joke Epicurus took the liberty of making on Plato and the Platonists; he called them Dionysiokolakes.” In its original sense the word signifies “Flatterers of Dionysius”—tyrants’ accessories and lick-spittles; but it is also as much as to say, “They are all ACTORS, there is nothing genuine about them.” Epicurus was annoyed by the grandiose manner, the mise en scène style of which Plato and his scholars were masters. The old school-teacher of Samos, concealed in his little garden, cast the malignant reproach that the great idealists were performers enjoying the show of their own ideals. Philosophers can be more stinging than any other class of men.

Key Insight: Philosophers are often actors who enjoy the theatrical presentation of their own ideals more than they pursue anything genuine. The malice and envy behind the masks reveal that “truth” frequently serves the drive to pose as superior or to entertain oneself with the grandeur of one’s own constructions.

Connection to the book: This sharpens the psychological portrait begun in the Preface (philosophy as caricature) and Section 6 (philosophy as confession). It humanizes and de-deifies the history of philosophy, clearing space for the new philosophers who will be more honest about their drives. It anticipates the critique of the “actor” in later sections and the demand for a nobility that no longer needs to perform its superiority.

Why it matters: Intellectual and moral authorities still perform sincerity while enjoying the spectacle of their own ideals—on stage, in media, in academia, in politics. For readers of the Kaufmann translation, Nietzsche’s sting invites us to ask: Where is the actor in this performance of truth? What pleasure is the philosopher (or public thinker) taking in the mise en scène of their own convictions? The free spirit learns to enjoy the show without being taken in by it.
Companion Note: Philosophers as actors enjoying their own ideals—this is one of Nietzsche’s most humanizing and deflationary insights. Use the slides to visualize the mask and the garden-god’s sting; then return to the Kaufmann text ready to spot the performance in even the most solemn systems.
