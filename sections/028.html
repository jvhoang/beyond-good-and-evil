FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 28

Identifier: 28 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
What is most difficult to render from one language into another is the tempo of its style, which has its basis in the character of the race, or to speak more physiologically, in the average tempo of its metabolism. There are honestly meant translations that, as involuntary vulgarizations, are almost falsifications of the original merely because its bold and merry tempo (which leaps over and obviates all dangers in things and words) could not be translated.

A German is almost incapable of presto in his language; thus also, as may be reasonably inferred, of many of the most delightful and daring nuances of free, free-spirited thought. And just as the buffoon and satyr are foreign to him in body and conscience, so Aristophanes and Petronius are untranslatable for him. Everything ponderous, viscous, and solemnly clumsy, all long-winded and boring types of style are developed in profuse variety among Germans—forgive me the fact that even Goethe’s prose, in its mixture of stiffness and elegance, is no exception, being a reflection of the “good old time” to which it belongs, and a reflection of German taste at a time when there still was a “German taste”—a rococo taste in moribus et artibus.

Lessing is an exception, owing to his histrionic nature which understood much and understood how to do many things. He was not the translator of Bayle for nothing and liked to flee to the neighborhood of Diderot and Voltaire, and better yet—that of the Roman comedy writers. In tempo, too, Lessing loved free thinking and escape from Germany.

But how could the German language, even in the prose of a Lessing, imitate the tempo of Machiavelli, who in his Principe lets us breathe the dry, refined air of Florence and cannot help presenting the most serious matters in a boisterous allegrissimo, perhaps not without a malicious artistic sense of the contrast he risks—long, difficult, hard, dangerous thoughts and the tempo of the gallop and the very best, most capricious humor?

Key Takeaway: Style’s tempo is not superficial ornament but the physiological signature of a people’s character and metabolism. Some languages and national characters are constitutionally incapable of the swift, dangerous, capricious freedom of thought that others (Machiavelli’s Italian, the Greek of Aristophanes) embody naturally. Translation and even reading across tempo differences inevitably falsify.

Connections to the broader BGE project: Extends the linguistic/grammatical critique of Section 20 into physiology and national character. The free spirit must be aware that even the form of thought (its speed, humor, danger) is racially and metabolically conditioned. This prepares the later emphasis on breeding, rank, and the exceptional individual who can transcend or combine tempos.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Read for the sheer pleasure of the examples. Nietzsche is showing his own ideal tempo: the allegrissimo of Machiavelli. The aside about Goethe and “German taste” is affectionate criticism.

Why it matters today: In globalized English-dominated discourse and machine translation, the loss of native tempos is constant. Political and philosophical styles (ironic French, ponderous German academic, rapid Anglo-American) carry hidden physiological and cultural assumptions. Nietzsche reminds us that some thoughts can only be thought at certain speeds and in certain linguistic bodies.
Companion Polish: A masterclass in stylistic physiology. The free spirit learns to hear the tempo behind the words and to value those rare styles (and thinkers) capable of the gallop.
