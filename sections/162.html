FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 162

The Neighbor and the Common Good
Identifier: 162
Simple modern summary

When everyone repeats that something is good, the word starts to sound empty and fake. Nietzsche also rejects the idea of a true common good—what is shared by everyone is usually small and watered down. Real value is rarely what the whole crowd can agree on.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Nietzsche writes: “'Good' is no longer good when one's neighbor mouths it. And how should there be a 'common good'! The term contradicts itself: whatever can be common is always of small value.”

The moment a value is taken up and repeated by the neighbor—by the many—it loses its distinction and its power. What is shouted in the marketplace or shared in the herd is already cheapened. The “common good” is therefore a contradiction: the good that can be possessed and affirmed by all is, by that very fact, mediocre and low in rank.

This epigram targets the democratic and Christian instinct that seeks the good in what unites or can be shared by everyone. Nietzsche counters that the highest goods are rare, unshareable, and aristocratic by nature. The will to be oneself—the will to power—drives toward distinction, not consensus. To make the good common is to make it small.

Key Insight: True value resists popularization. The free spirit refuses to measure worth by how widely something can be affirmed or consumed. The neighbor’s mouth is the graveyard of nobility.

Modern companion insight: In an age of viral consensus, social media likes, and political appeals to the “common good,” Nietzsche’s warning is acute. The pressure to align with what “everyone” can endorse flattens excellence and protects mediocrity. The free spirit cultivates values that may remain incomprehensible or offensive to the neighbor, precisely because they are not meant to be common property.
Key Context: Epigram exposing the leveling logic of herd morality and neighbor-love in Part Four. Prepares revaluation of what counts as “good.”
