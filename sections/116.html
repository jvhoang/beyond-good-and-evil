FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 116

Rebaptizing Our Badness
Identifier: 116
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 116 offers one of Nietzsche’s most liberating epigrams: “The great epochs of our life are at the points when we gain courage to rebaptize our badness as the best in us.”

Great turning points occur not through moral improvement in the conventional sense, but when we find the courage to reinterpret what we and others have labeled “bad,” “evil,” or “flawed” as precisely the source of our strength, creativity, and vitality. The act of rebaptizing—renaming—is itself an act of power and self-overcoming.

Key Insight: What society or our own conscience condemns as our worst qualities may be the very materials out of which our highest achievements are built. The courage to revalue is the mark of higher types.

Connection to BGE themes: This is a direct expression of the revaluation of all values. It embodies the will to power as creative reinterpretation rather than mere domination. The free spirit refuses inherited moral labels and dares to affirm the “immoral” drives that actually sustain life and greatness. It rejects the slave morality that requires us to despise our strongest instincts.

Modern companion insight: In 2026, therapeutic culture, social media shame cycles, and identity politics often demand public repentance for “problematic” traits or histories. Self-help frequently pathologizes ambition, competitiveness, or unconventional desires. The free spirit practices the courage to privately and selectively rebaptize: the restlessness once called “ADHD” becomes creative drive; the “toxic” directness becomes necessary honesty; the “selfish” focus becomes the condition for real contribution. The task is not to whitewash vice but to honestly assess which of our so-called bad qualities serve life and excellence when properly directed.

This epigram invites a personal and cultural courage that is rare and dangerous.
Key Context: Epigram celebrating the creative power of revaluation in Part Four. Central to Nietzsche’s project of moving beyond good and evil through self-overcoming.
