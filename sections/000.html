FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Preface

Identifier: Preface
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
In the broader context of Friedrich Nietzsche's philosophy, the Preface to Beyond Good and Evil (1886) marks a pivotal moment in his intellectual development. Following the poetic and prophetic Thus Spoke Zarathustra (1883-1885), BGE represents Nietzsche's return to a more aphoristic, critical, and diagnostic mode of writing. It serves as a "prelude to a philosophy of the future," systematically dismantling the foundational assumptions of Western metaphysics, epistemology, and morality while preparing the terrain for his positive doctrines of the will to power, perspectivism, and the revaluation of all values. Nietzsche positions himself as a "good European" and "free spirit" who inherits the spiritual tension created by the historical struggle against dogmatism.



The main argument of the Preface is encapsulated in its provocative opening supposition: "SUPPOSING that Truth is a woman—what then?" Nietzsche uses this gendered metaphor to indict dogmatic philosophers as clumsy, serious, and importunate suitors who have failed to understand or win "her" affections through their awkward methods. Dogmatic philosophy—exemplified by Plato's positing of Pure Spirit and the Good in Itself—is portrayed not as timeless wisdom but as a "noble puerilism and tyronism," built upon popular superstitions (such as the soul or ego), grammatical deceptions, and audacious generalizations from personal, all-too-human experiences. Great systems like the Vedanta in Asia and Platonism in Europe are seen as awe-inspiring caricatures necessary for ideas to inscribe themselves on humanity's heart, much like astrology preceded astronomy.



Nietzsche acknowledges a debt to this dogmatism: the struggle against Plato (and its popular manifestation, Christianity, which he calls "Platonism for the 'people'") generated an unprecedented "magnificent tension of soul" in Europe. This tension, like a strained bow, enables aiming at the furthest goals. Attempts to relax it—through Jesuitism or democratic enlightenment (with its press and newspapers)—are rejected. Instead, "we GOOD EUROPEANS, and free, VERY free spirits" retain the distress and the tension, along with "perhaps also the arrow, the duty, and, who knows? THE GOAL TO AIM AT."



Key quotes include the opening supposition about Truth as a woman; the description of dogmatic philosophy as caricatures; "It amounted to the very inversion of truth, and the denial of the PERSPECTIVE—the fundamental condition—of life, to speak of Spirit and the Good as Plato spoke of them"; and the closing vision of the tense bow. The dated signature "Sils Maria Upper Engadine, JUNE, 1885" anchors it personally.



Implications for morality, truth, and power are profound. By questioning the "will to truth" itself and suggesting dogmatism's foundations are suspect, Nietzsche challenges absolute moral systems derived from metaphysical "truths." Truth becomes perspectival and life-serving rather than an objective end. Power shifts from dogmatic imposition to the creative tension and affirmative will of the free spirit who can aim beyond good and evil. This undermines traditional morality's claims to universality and prepares the critique of slave morality versus noble values.



In the architecture of BGE, the Preface sets the stage for Part One's detailed assault on the "Prejudices of Philosophers." It introduces the free spirit persona, the critique of opposites and values, and the call for new philosophers. It frames the entire book as an act of wakefulness and risk-taking, contrasting with the somnolence of traditional systems. The Preface's emphasis on perspective, caricature, and the bow's tension resonates throughout subsequent sections on the will to truth (Section 1), belief in antitheses (Section 2), instinctual thinking (Section 3), and the affirmation of untruth as a condition of life (Section 4), culminating in the psychological and naturalis tic critiques that follow. It establishes BGE as both destructive of old idols and pregnant with future possibilities.
