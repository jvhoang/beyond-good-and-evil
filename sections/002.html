FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 2

Identifier: 2
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 2 continues the assault by attacking the “fundamental belief of metaphysicians”: THE BELIEF IN ANTITHESES OF VALUES. For centuries philosophers have assumed that truth cannot come from error, generosity from selfishness, or the pure vision of the wise from covetousness. Such genesis from opposites is, they insist, impossible. Highest values must have an origin of their own—in the lap of Being, in the “Thing-in-itself.”

Nietzsche asks us to doubt this on the very threshold where doubt is most necessary. It may be that the popular valuations and antitheses are merely “superficial estimates, merely provisional perspectives,” seen from some corner, “perhaps from below—‘frog perspectives,’” as painters say. It might even be possible that what constitutes the value of the good and respected things “consists precisely in their being insidiously related, knotted, and crocheted to these evil and apparently opposed things—perhaps even in being essentially identical with them.”

Key Insight for the book: The highest values may draw their strength precisely from what traditional morality calls their opposite. The “evil” may be more fundamental to life than the “good.”

How it connects: This suspicion of antitheses directly extends the Preface’s critique of dogmatism and Section 1’s questioning of the will to truth. It prepares the naturalization of consciousness in Section 3 and the explicit affirmation of untruth in Section 4.

Why it matters today: Our public life is still dominated by binary thinking—left versus right, truth versus lies, us versus them. Nietzsche invites us to examine whether these clean oppositions are honest descriptions or “frog perspectives” that serve power while obscuring the more complex, knotted reality of human motivation and value.
Companion Note: The “philosophers of the dangerous ‘Perhaps’” are those willing to entertain that what we call evil may be more valuable for life than what we call good.
