FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 2

Identifier: 2
Simple modern summary

Philosophers assume good and evil are totally opposite and cannot come from the same source. Nietzsche says that clean split might be fake—what we call good might actually be tied to what we call bad. Our moral binaries might just be a limited view, not the full picture.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 2 targets the “fundamental belief of metaphysicians”: THE BELIEF IN ANTITHESES OF VALUES. Philosophers have long assumed that truth cannot arise from error, generosity from selfishness, or the pure vision of the wise from base covetousness. Such origins in opposites are, they insist, impossible. Highest values must come from a realm of their own—in the lap of Being, in the “Thing-in-itself.” Nietzsche urges us to doubt precisely where doubt has been most forbidden—right at the threshold. The popular valuations and antitheses may be nothing more than “superficial estimates, merely provisional perspectives,” seen from some corner, “perhaps from below—‘frog perspectives,’” as painters say. More radically: the value of the “good and respected things” may consist “precisely in their being insidiously related, knotted, and crocheted to these evil and apparently opposed things—perhaps even in being essentially identical with them.”

Key Insight: The highest values may draw their strength precisely from what traditional morality labels their opposite. What we call “evil” may be more fundamental to life than what we call “good.” The clean division of values is a “frog perspective” that conceals their entangled, interdependent origins.

Connection to the book: This suspicion of clean antitheses extends the Preface’s critique of dogmatism and Section 1’s interrogation of the will to truth. It prepares the ground for naturalizing consciousness (Section 3), affirming untruth as a condition of life (Section 4), and the broader revaluation of values throughout the book. The “philosophers of the dangerous ‘Perhaps’” learn to think without the safety net of metaphysical dualisms.

Why it matters: Public discourse remains addicted to binary oppositions—left versus right, truth versus lies, us versus them. Nietzsche invites readers of the Kaufmann translation to test whether these clean divisions are honest maps of reality or “frog perspectives” that serve power while hiding the far more tangled, interdependent nature of human motives and values. The real value of an idea may lie precisely in what it seems to oppose.
Companion Note: The true “philosophers of the dangerous ‘Perhaps’” are willing to consider that what we call evil may be more valuable for life than what we call good.
