FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 15

Identifier: 15 • Physiology and Sense Organs • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
In the broader context of Friedrich Nietzsche's Beyond Good and Evil (1886), Section 15 is a pivotal moment in Part One, "On the Prejudices of Philosophers." Following the Preface's attack on dogmatic philosophy as "noble puerilism" and the systematic exposure in sections 1–14 of the will to truth, belief in value-antitheses, the instinctive basis of conscious thought, untruth as a condition of life, and philosophy as unconscious autobiography, Nietzsche turns here to the status of our sense-organs and the external world itself. This section naturalizes epistemology and continues the book's project of a "prelude to a philosophy of the future" by insisting that even physiology must be conducted with philosophical conscience — from inside the world rather than from some impossible outside vantage.

The core aphorism is a sharp, almost playful reductio aimed at idealist accounts (Berkeley, Schopenhauer) that treat sense-organs as mere "phenomena" or representations without genuine causal power. "To study physiology with a clear conscience, one must insist on the fact that the sense-organs are not phenomena in the sense of the idealistic philosophy; as such they certainly could not be causes! Sensualism, therefore, at least as regulative hypothesis, if not as heuristic principle." Nietzsche then dismantles the opposite extreme: "What? And others say even that the external world is the work of our organs? But then our body, as a part of this external world, would be the work of our organs! But then our organs themselves would be the work of our organs! It seems to me that this is a complete REDUCTIO AD ABSURDUM, if the conception CAUSA SUI is something fundamentally absurd. Consequently, the external world is NOT the work of our organs—?"

Philosophical implications for truth/morality/power/free spirit: The section affirms an embodied, immanent perspectivism. We are not outside observers constructing or mirroring a world; our sense-organs are real participants and causes within the very reality they help disclose. This rejects both naive realism (the world simply "is" as sensed without interpretation) and the idealist move that reduces the world to our organs' products (leading to the self-undermining absurdity of causa sui). Truth and knowledge are always from a specific, interested, physiological standpoint inside the world. For morality, it undercuts any claim to "pure," disinterested, or otherworldly knowledge upon which traditional ascetic or dogmatic moralities rest. Power lies in the courage to affirm our embeddedness and the interpretive strength of our particular perspective rather than fleeing into metaphysical fictions of a "beyond." It advances BGE by extending the suspicion of dogmatism and "causa sui" fictions even to the tools of knowing, preparing the free spirit's experimental, life-affirming mode of interpretation.
Key Context: Part One of BGE. Emphasizes perspectivism, embodied knowledge, will to power as interpretive force, and the revaluation of values against idealist and dogmatic prejudices.
