FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 145

Adornment and the Secondary Role
Identifier: 145
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 145 states: “Comparing man and woman generally, one may say that woman would not have the genius for adornment, if she had not the instinct for the SECONDARY role.”

Nietzsche isolates a specific feminine genius: the capacity for adornment, ornament, and the art of making appearance captivating. This talent, he suggests, is inseparable from an instinct for playing the secondary rather than the primary role. Adornment is not mere vanity or submission; it is an active art of framing, enhancing, and directing attention—often toward the man or the scene rather than claiming the center for oneself. The woman who excels at this instinctively understands how to be seen without being the sole focus.

The observation refuses simple hierarchy. It acknowledges a genuine artistic and psychological capacity that men typically lack or possess in lesser degree. At the same time it ties that capacity to a preference for the complementary position. In Nietzsche’s typology, this is not a defect but a difference in strategy and power expression. The adorned woman exercises influence through indirection, taste, and the creation of atmosphere rather than direct command.

Key Insight: The genius for adornment is rooted in an instinct for the secondary role; one enables the other and gives it distinctive power.

Connection to BGE themes: Part Four repeatedly examines sexual difference without moralizing. This epigram complements observations on women’s scorn for “woman,” different emotional tempi, and the uses of vanity. It belongs to Nietzsche’s project of seeing through democratic and egalitarian simplifications to the actual economies of strength and style between types. The free spirit learns to appreciate such differential gifts rather than flatten them into sameness.

Modern companion insight: In 2026, fashion, beauty industries, Instagram aesthetics, and even corporate “personal branding” for women still demonstrate extraordinary investment in adornment and presentation that exceeds most men’s efforts. Contemporary ideology often frames this as internalized oppression or false consciousness. The free spirit considers whether it also reflects a persistent instinct for the secondary role that carries its own form of power and pleasure. Rejecting both compulsory adornment and the puritanical demand that women renounce it, one can recognize the art without pretending the instinct is identical in both sexes or that it can be willed away by slogans. Different roles and different geniuses persist beneath surface equality.
Key Context: Epigram on adornment as expression of the secondary instinct. Part Four typology of sexual difference and styles of power.
