FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 37

Identifier: 37 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
“What? Doesn't this mean, to speak with the vulgar: God is refuted, but the devil is not?” On the contrary! On the contrary, my friends! And the devil—who forces you to speak with the vulgar?

Key Takeaway: The hypothesis of the world as will to power (or will affecting will) does not leave a “devil” (evil, the negative, destruction) standing after “God” (the good, the positive, creation) has been refuted. The vulgar moralistic framing that expects a residual “devil” is itself rejected. The will to power is beyond the God/devil, good/evil dichotomy.

Connections to the broader BGE project: A direct, mocking response to the moralistic misreading of the will-to-power hypothesis. It reinforces that the revaluation is not the replacement of one moral god with a demonic one but the overcoming of the entire moral framework. Ties back to the Preface and Part One’s exposure of moral prejudices.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): The tone is exasperated and playful. Nietzsche refuses the moralistic trap of the question. The “On the contrary!” is emphatic refusal of the entire framing.

Why it matters today: Popular and academic receptions of Nietzsche (or of power-centered thinking in general) often reduce it to “might makes right” or “the devil wins.” This section insists on a genuinely extra-moral reading: power is not the new “bad” after the old “good” is gone; the categories themselves are the problem.
Companion Polish: A swift slap at moralistic misreadings. The will to power is not the victory of the devil; it is the end of the God/devil game.
