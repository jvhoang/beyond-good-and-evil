FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 23

Identifier: 23
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
All psychology hitherto has run aground on moral prejudices and timidities; it has not dared to launch out into the depths. In so far as one can recognize in what has been written evidence of what has been kept silent, nobody had yet harboured the notion of psychology as the Morphology and DEVELOPMENT-DOCTRINE OF THE WILL TO POWER, as Nietzsche conceives it. The power of moral prejudices has penetrated deeply into the most intellectual world and has operated in an injurious, obstructive, blinding, and distorting manner.

Breakdown of the argument:
• A proper physio-psychology has to contend with unconscious antagonism in the heart of the investigator; it has “the heart” against it. Even a doctrine of the reciprocal conditionalness of the “good” and the “bad” impulses causes distress in a still strong and manly conscience.
• Still more so a doctrine of the derivation of all good impulses from bad ones. If one regards even hatred, envy, covetousness, and imperiousness as life-conditioning emotions that must be present if life is to be further developed, one will suffer from such a view as from sea-sickness.
• Yet this hypothesis is far from the strangest and most painful in this immense, almost new domain of dangerous knowledge. There are a hundred good reasons why everyone should keep away from it who can. But if one has once drifted hither with one’s bark—well! very good! Now let us set our teeth firmly, open our eyes, and keep our hand fast on the helm! We sail away right OVER morality; we crush out, we destroy perhaps the remains of our own morality by daring to make our voyage thither—but what do WE matter.

Key Takeaway: Psychology must become a ruthless physio-psychology of the will to power. It must translate man back into nature and treat even the “immoral” emotions as necessary conditions of life rather than moral failings. This is the culminating psychological turn of Part One and the bridge to the free spirit and philosophers of the future.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This section gathers and radicalizes all the preceding critiques (of the I, causa sui, natural law, etc.). It names explicitly what the book has been practicing: a morphology and developmental doctrine of the will to power. It prepares Part Two and the later Genealogy of Morals.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): This is one of the most stirring passages in BGE. Read it aloud. Feel the shift from diagnostic critique to the heroic, almost seafaring call to adventure. The sea-sickness image is perfect: genuine insight into the depths makes one physically ill at first.

Why it matters today: Depth psychology, evolutionary psychology, and affective neuroscience are belatedly doing some of this work, often still under moralistic or therapeutic disguises. Nietzsche offers a cleaner, more dangerous, and ultimately more life-affirming frame: stop pathologizing the conditions of life and learn to sail over them with open eyes.
Companion Polish: Section 23 is the trumpet call that ends Part One. The free spirit is not the one who has nicer thoughts; it is the one who has the courage and the stomach to voyage into the dangerous knowledge of the will to power without demanding new moral certainties in return.
