FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 23

Identifier: 23
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
All psychology hitherto has run aground on moral prejudices and timidities; it has not dared to launch out into the depths. In so far as it is allowable to recognize in that which has hitherto been written evidence of that which has hitherto been kept silent, nobody had yet harboured the notion of psychology as the Morphology and Development-Doctrine of the Will to Power, as Nietzsche conceives of it.

The power of moral prejudices has penetrated deeply into the most intellectual world. A proper physio-psychology has to contend with unconscious antagonism in the heart of the investigator. Even a doctrine of the reciprocal conditionalness of the 'good' and the 'bad' impulses causes distress. If one regards the emotions of hatred, envy, covetousness, and imperiousness as life-conditioning emotions, as factors which must be present fundamentally in the general economy of life, one will suffer from such a view as from sea-sickness. Yet this hypothesis is far from being the strangest in this immense and almost new domain of dangerous knowledge.

Key Takeaway: Psychology must become a morphology and genealogy of will to power, translating moral phenomena back into natural, physiological, and power-dynamic terms without moralizing. The 'good' impulses may derive from 'bad' ones; the task is not condemnation but understanding the economy of life.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This is the culminating psychological turn of Part One. It announces the program that will be executed in the Genealogy of Morals and later works. It completes the critique of previous philosophers by showing that even their 'psychology' was moral prejudice in disguise. It opens the way for the free spirit who can sail 'over morality.'

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Note the nautical metaphors ('drifted hither with one's bark,' 'set our teeth firmly,' 'open our eyes,' 'sail away right OVER morality'). The section is both warning and invitation. The 'we' who undertake this voyage accept that it may 'crush out the remains of our own morality' — and ask 'but what do WE matter.'

Why it matters today: In evolutionary psychology, behavioral economics, moral psychology (Haidt, Greene), and even corporate 'organizational behavior,' we see attempts to naturalize morality and motivation. Nietzsche's challenge remains: can we do so without new moral prejudices or without losing the courage to face the 'dangerous knowledge' that our highest values may be life-serving but not 'true' in any absolute sense? Essential for any honest self-understanding or social science.
Companion Polish: This section is the bridge from critique to positive project. The free spirit does not merely expose prejudices but risks the voyage into physio-psychology of power, accepting the personal cost. It is both exhilarating and nauseating — the true test of intellectual honesty.
