FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 56

Pessimism and the Ideal of Eternal Recurrence
Identifier: 56
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
In the broader context of Nietzsche's philosophy, Section 56 is one of the most positive and prophetic moments in Part Three of Beyond Good and Evil. Prompted by an enigmatical desire, Nietzsche describes his long endeavor to go to the bottom of pessimism, freeing it from the half-Christian, half-German narrowness and stupidity in which it presented itself in Schopenhauer's philosophy. With an "Asiatic and super-Asiatic eye," he looked into the most world-renouncing of all possible modes of thought—beyond good and evil, no longer under the dominion and delusion of morality (unlike Buddha and Schopenhauer). This opens his eyes (without really desiring it) to the opposite ideal: the ideal of the most world-approving, exuberant, and vivacious man who has not only learnt to compromise and arrange with that which was and is, but wishes to have it again AS IT WAS AND IS, for all eternity, insatiably calling out da capo, not only to himself, but to the whole piece and play; and not only the play, but actually to him who requires the play—and makes it necessary; because he always requires himself anew—and makes himself necessary. "What? And this would not be—circulus vitiosus deus?"

This section directly follows the ladder of cruelty in 55, transforming nihilism into the highest affirmation. Key quotes: "Whoever, like myself, prompted by some enigmatical desire, has long endeavoured to go to the bottom of the question of pessimism..." "the opposite ideal: the ideal of the most world-approving, exuberant, and vivacious man..." "insatiably calling out de capo... What? And this would not be—circulus vitiosus deus?"

Implications: Pessimism and nihilism can be overcome not by escape or new theism but by the highest affirmation that loves fate (amor fati) and wills the eternal return of all, including suffering. This is the ultimate revaluation: life justified aesthetically and ontologically as will to power that requires itself. It embodies the rising generation's response to the sacrifice of God for nothingness. For the free spirit, it is the most world-affirming spirituality.
