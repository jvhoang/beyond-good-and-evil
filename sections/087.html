FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 87

Fettered Heart, Free Spirit
Identifier: 87
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
The epigram states that when one firmly fetters the heart (passions and attachments) and keeps it prisoner, one can allow the spirit many liberties. Nietzsche notes he has said this before, but people believe it only if they already know it. In Part Four's interludes, it distills a central BGE motif: rigorous self-discipline and even "unfreedom" of desire as the precondition for intellectual freedom, experimentation, and perspectivism. It contrasts with modern ideals of liberation and prepares the ground for the free spirit who achieves range through self-mastery rather than indulgence. The remark underscores that true freedom of mind is acquired, not natural, aligning with the book's call for new philosophers of dangerous "Perhaps." ~170 words.
