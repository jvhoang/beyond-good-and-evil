FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 90

How to Make Oneself Smaller
Identifier: 90
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
The epigram: "To make oneself smaller than one is—that is a trick that everyone knows; everyone has practiced it."

Nietzsche identifies a universal social tactic: deliberate self-miniaturization to fit in, to be liked, to avoid friction. It is not modesty but a calculated diminution. By shrinking our presentation of self we make social commerce easier and reduce the threat that our full stature or complexity might pose to others.

Key Insight: The performance of smallness is a widespread, learned skill used to ease social navigation at the expense of authenticity and stature.

Modern companion insight: This epigram diagnoses the social media era's pressure to flatten the self into palatable fragments. Everyone knows how to crop their own image—literal and figurative—to become less imposing, less contradictory, less demanding. The cost is the same: the full person disappears behind the miniature that travels well. The free spirit refuses to practice the trick on principle; better solitude or honest friction than the comfortable lie of diminished stature.
