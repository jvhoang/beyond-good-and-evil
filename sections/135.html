FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 135

Pharisaism as Condition of Being Good
Identifier: 135
Simple modern summary

Pharisaism is not always hypocrisy ruining goodness—sometimes it is the scaffolding that keeps goodness going. A little self-presentation helps imperfect people stay moral. Total transparency can collapse ethical effort.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Context

Section 135 states: “Pharisaism is not a deterioration of the good man; a considerable part of it is rather an essential condition of being good.”

Key insight

The outward show, the careful maintenance of reputation, the selective blindness to one’s own flaws—these “pharisaical” elements are not mere hypocrisy that ruins goodness. They are often the necessary scaffolding that allows imperfect humans to sustain moral effort, social trust, and self-respect long enough to actually do good. Without some appearance-management, the fragile project of being good collapses under the weight of total transparency or self-knowledge. A measure of outward piety or self-deception can be structurally required to maintain real ethical conduct.

BGE connection

Nietzsche continues his exposure of the psychological mechanisms behind morality. “Goodness” is not a pure inner state but a social and self-regulatory performance that requires masks. The free spirit sees this without moral outrage—recognizing that complete honesty about one’s motives would often paralyze action. Revaluation distinguishes useful illusions from life-denying ones.

Modern companion

In 2026 corporate ESG reports, political virtue signaling, academic diversity statements, and personal branding on social platforms often function exactly as Pharisaism: the necessary public face that enables continued institutional power or personal functioning. The free spirit neither condemns all performance nor mistakes it for the whole truth. One cultivates enough honest self-sight to avoid self-righteousness while accepting that some presentational discipline is required to keep doing the work one values. Total anti-Pharisaism can be as destructive as total hypocrisy.

Key Context: Epigram on the functional role of hypocrisy in morality. Part Four realism about ethical psychology.
