FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 171

Pity Ludicrous to the Man of Knowledge
Identifier: 171
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
“Pity in a man of knowledge seems almost ludicrous, like sensitive hands on a Cyclops.”

Nietzsche casts pity as out of place—indeed, comically mismatched—when directed at the knower. The Cyclops, a figure of raw power and singular vision, has no use for tender, soft hands; such gestures appear ridiculous, almost insulting to his nature. The man of knowledge, similarly, requires a certain hardness, distance, and severity. Pity, with its softening, presuming intimacy, undermines the very qualities needed for genuine insight.

This epigram continues Nietzsche’s recurring critique of pity (Mitleid) as a dubious virtue. Far from being generous or noble, pity often signals weakness in both the pitier and the pitied. It multiplies suffering rather than alleviating the conditions that make it necessary. For the psychologist and free spirit, pity must be overcome or transformed; unchecked, it flattens distinctions of rank and discourages the self-overcoming that produces higher types.

Key Insight: Pity is not an appropriate response to the exceptional or the knowing; it is an intrusion of the herd’s sentimentality upon those who must see clearly and act with strength. The knower’s task demands something sterner than compassion as ordinarily understood.

BGE connection: In the interludes of Part Four, Nietzsche sharpens his aphoristic scalpel against modern moral instincts. This line prepares themes developed later: the “pathos of distance,” the suspicion of egalitarian pity, and the call for a revaluation that distinguishes noble hardness from slave morality’s softening.

Modern companion insight: Contemporary culture prizes empathy and “safe spaces” of feeling. The free spirit learns to distinguish genuine helpfulness from the enervating pity that keeps people (and oneself) small. Study the Cyclops: one-eyed, unblinking focus on what is, rather than what one wishes to feel about it. Knowledge sometimes requires the refusal of tender hands.
