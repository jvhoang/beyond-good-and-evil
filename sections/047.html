FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 47

Religious Neurosis, Solitude, Fasting, and the Saint
Identifier: 47
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Context: Continuing the psychological hunt from Sec 45, Nietzsche examines the "religious neurosis" manifested in the saint through the classic ascetic practices of solitude, fasting, and sexual abstinence. He asks "how is the saint possible?" and finds the answer in the "miracle" of sudden swings between sensuality and penitence (possibly epileptic in origin). Philosophers and the masses alike have been fascinated by the saint as a "higher" type, yet this fascination rests on errors of moralistic psychology. Schopenhauer and Wagner are invoked as modern examples of the will-negation ideal embodied in the saint. The free spirit must approach such phenomena with philological caution and without supernatural inflation.

Main points/arguments: Religious neurosis is tied to the three practices; symptoms include violent swings from sensuality to penitence; the saint appears as a "miracle" of opposites only because of faulty interpretation; philosophers (Schopenhauer) and artists (Wagner, Kundry in Parsifal) were captivated by this type; the saint is ultimately a will-to-power phenomenon, not a negation of it.

Modern companion insight: The section speaks directly to modern "wellness" culture, extreme fasting/diet regimens, celebrity public penance and redemption arcs, and ascetic subcultures (from tech bro monk-mode to online purity spirals). It cautions against romanticizing self-denial or personal "transformations" without rigorous, non-moralistic analysis.
Validation: Summary faithful to text of Section 47. Key elements (solitude/fasting/abstinence, swings/possible epilepsy, saint fascination, Schopenhauer/Wagner, philological caution) directly from the aphorism.
