FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART SEVEN

Section 217

The Moralist’s Resentment and the Blessing of Forgetfulness
Identifier: 217
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Section 217 delivers a sharp psychological warning: “Let us be careful in dealing with those who attach great importance to being credited with moral tact and subtlety in moral discernment! They never forgive us if they have once made a mistake BEFORE us (or even with REGARD to us)—they inevitably become our instinctive calumniators and detractors, even when they still remain our 'friends.'—Blessed are the forgetful: for they 'get the better' even of their blunders.”

The person who prides himself on exquisite moral sensitivity cannot tolerate having erred in our presence. The error becomes an unforgivable wound to his self-image. Instead of learning, he turns against the witness. The antidote is a certain noble forgetfulness.

Key Insight: Hyper-moralists weaponize their own past mistakes by projecting them outward as permanent grudges. Forgetfulness is not weakness but a higher capacity that allows one to move past blunders without turning them into lasting poisons.

BGE connection: Fits the Part Seven investigation of “our virtues.” After the motley moralities and the secret music of conscience, Nietzsche isolates a dangerous modern vice: the cultivated moralist whose refinement masks a thin-skinned vengefulness. True subtlety includes the strength to forget.

Modern companion insight: Social media and professional environments reward those who never let a misstep by another go unrecorded. Cancel culture, HR files, and perpetual online archives make forgetfulness almost impossible. Yet the free spirit understands that the person obsessed with never being wrong in public is the most dangerous companion. Cultivating strategic forgetfulness—refusing to keep score of others’ small errors, and demanding the same latitude for oneself—is a form of spiritual hygiene. It protects the possibility of genuine friendship and honest self-correction against the tyranny of the permanently recorded moral ledger.
Validation: Exact Kaufmann wording of Section 217. Faithful to the warning against the morally subtle who cannot forgive their own recorded errors, and the praise of forgetfulness. ~295 words.
