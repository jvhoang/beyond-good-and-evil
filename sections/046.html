FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 46

The Faith of Pascal and the Suicide of Reason
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
In the broader context of Nietzsche's thought, Section 46 builds on Section 45's call for profound psychological investigation, focusing on the specific character of early Christian faith as distinct from northern Protestant 'slave-faith.' Nietzsche continues his genealogy of morals and values by tracing Christianity's origins to an Oriental slave revenge against Roman noble toleration. This fits BGE's project of unmasking the 'transvaluation of all ancient values' achieved by Christianity, contrasting with the free spirit's task of a new, life-affirming revaluation. Main points/arguments: The faith early Christianity achieved in a skeptical Roman world is not austere slave-faith (Luther/Cromwell) but the faith of Pascal—a 'continuous suicide of reason,' a tough worm-like reason not slain at once. Christian faith from the beginning is sacrifice of all freedom, pride, self-confidence of spirit; subjection, self-derision, self-mutilation. It contains 'cruelty and religious Phoenicianism' adapted to a tender, fastidious conscience. The formula 'God on the Cross' represents unparalleled boldness of inversion, promising transvaluation. It was the Oriental slave taking revenge on Rome's 'Catholicism' of non-faith. Enlightenment causes revolt because the slave desires the unconditioned and tyrannous without nuance. Skepticism regarding suffering (aristocratic) was a cause of the French Revolution as last great slave-insurrection. Key Kaufmann quotes: 'this faith is NOT that sincere, austere slave-faith... it is much rather the faith of Pascal, which resembles in a terrible manner a continuous suicide of reason.' 'The Christian faith from the beginning, is sacrifice the sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-confidence of spirit.' '"God on the Cross"... It was the Orient, the PROFOUND Orient, it was the Oriental slave who thus took revenge on Rome.' 'The skepticism with regard to suffering, fundamentally only an attitude of aristocratic morality, was not the least of the causes... of the last great slave-insurrection which began with the French Revolution.' Implications for religion/morality: Exposes Christianity's core as life-denying cruelty and self-torment that inverts noble values, glorifying suffering and submission while resenting strength and nuance. Morality becomes tied to this 'suicide of reason' and slave psychology. Power is exercised through the weak's revenge via moral transvaluation. Role in BGE's overall project: Provides historical-psychological ground for the later sections (55, 62) on the ladder of cruelty and Christianity's presumption. It advances the book's diagnostic of European soul-tension and prepares the free spirit's task to overcome this inheritance without naive enlightenment or return to barbarism. (Word count: ~480)
