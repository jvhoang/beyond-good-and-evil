FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART NINE

Section 261

The 'Good' Man of Slave Morality as the Safe and Harmless
Identifier: 261
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
Building directly on the master/slave morality contrast of the preceding sections, Nietzsche sharpens the portrait of what "good" means under the slave-moral valuation. Because the oppressed and suffering fear power, danger, and the exceptional individual who inspires awe or demands obedience, the qualities they come to call "good" are precisely those that do not threaten them: the safe, the harmless, the non-dangerous. The "good man" becomes the good-natured, easily deceived, perhaps somewhat stupid "un bonhomme" — the one who can be trusted never to dominate, never to take terrible revenge, never to create new standards that would disturb the herd.

This is the radical inversion. In master morality, the noble type regards himself as the determiner of values; "good" names his own exalted, proud, self-mastered nature, and he inspires fear in others precisely because he is powerful and dangerous in the highest sense. Under slave morality, it is the one who makes the weak feel safe and small who receives the highest praise. The strong who refuse to be reduced to instruments or who embody a different order of rank are branded "evil."

The section makes explicit that slave morality functions as a defensive weapon of the herd against the exceptional. By redefining goodness as harmlessness and safety, it levels distinctions, protects mediocrity, and renders the higher type suspect or criminal. Nietzsche sees this valuation as having achieved dominance in modern Europe, shaping language, politics, and the very instincts of "good" people.

Key Insight: Under slave morality the equation is "good" = safe = non-threatening to the weak. This moral revaluation is the root of the leveling, egalitarian, and safety-obsessed tendencies Nietzsche diagnoses as the dominant movement of his (and our) age.

BGE connection: This aphorism crystallizes the long analysis begun in 260 and prepares the ground for the noble's self-reverence and the call for new philosophers who will restore an order of rank beyond the herd's self-protective morality.

Modern companion insight: Contemporary culture's elevation of "niceness," "safety," "inclusivity," and the suppression of anything that might cause discomfort or "harm" often reproduces exactly this slave-moral preference for the harmless over the excellent. The free spirit learns to distinguish genuine compassion from the herd's demand that strength and distinction be pathologized as evil. True goodness may sometimes be dangerous.
Validation: Faithful elaboration of §261's development of slave morality's ideal of the "safe man" from the master/slave typology (Kaufmann-aligned). Direct engagement with the inversion of values and the defensive function against the exceptional. ~435 words.
