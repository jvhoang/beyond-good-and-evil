FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART FOUR

Section 157

Suicide as Consolation
Identifier: 157
Simple modern summary

Nietzsche says the thought that you could end your life can get you through terrible nights. It is not praise for suicide—it is about having an exit in mind when pain feels total. That mental escape can oddly restore enough calm to keep going.

EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 157 states: “The thought of suicide is a great consolation: by means of it one gets successfully through many a bad night.”

Nietzsche registers a psychological fact that is rarely acknowledged with such directness: the mere availability of the option to end one’s life functions as a powerful resource for endurance. It is not presented as a recommendation to act, but as an observation that the thought itself—having an exit, retaining ultimate sovereignty over one’s existence—provides comfort and strength sufficient to survive periods of acute suffering. The “bad night” is made survivable by the knowledge that one need not survive it if it becomes unbearable.

The epigram refuses both the moral panic that treats the thought as automatically pathological and the romanticization of suicide. It is a cool, almost medical note on one of the mind’s strategies for preserving the will to live under duress. The free spirit accepts this mechanism without condemnation or sentiment.

Key Insight: The possibility of voluntary exit is itself a support for continuing; sovereignty over death can sustain life.

Connection to BGE themes: This belongs to the book’s exploration of suffering, the tragic, the criminal, and the conditions under which higher types endure. It refuses the Christian and humanitarian idealization of life at all costs and anticipates Nietzsche’s later emphasis on the right to one’s own death. Part Four’s brevity allows the observation to stand without moralizing elaboration.

Modern companion insight: In 2026, mainstream mental health discourse and policy treat any contemplation of suicide as a crisis requiring intervention, often stripping individuals of autonomy in the name of protection. Assisted dying debates remain heavily restricted. The free spirit affirms the underlying psychological truth Nietzsche records: for some temperaments and in some circumstances, the retained option of exit is precisely what makes continued existence possible and dignified. One neither romanticizes suicide nor participates in the therapeutic state’s confiscation of ultimate self-ownership. The thought can be a tool; its existence need not lead to its use.
Key Context: Psychological observation on the consoling function of the thought of suicide. Part Four on suffering and autonomy.
