FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 20

Identifier: 20
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
In Part One's dismantling of philosophers' prejudices (right after the Preface's charge of "noble puerilism" in dogmatism and sections exposing the will to truth and the 'I' as fictions), Section 20 reveals a deeper layer: philosophical ideas are never "optional or autonomously evolving." They "grow up in connection and relationship with each other" like "the collective members of the fauna of a Continent." Under an "invisible spell," even the most independent thinkers "always revolve once more in the same orbit."

The core insight: "Their thinking is, in fact, far less a discovery than a re-recognizing, a remembering, a return and a home-coming to a far-off, ancient common-household of the soul." Philosophizing is "a kind of atavism of the highest order." Where there is "affinity of language," "everything is prepared at the outset for a similar development and succession of philosophical systems." The "spell of certain grammatical functions is ultimately also the spell of PHYSIOLOGICAL valuations and racial conditions." Philosophers in Ural-Altaic languages, where the subject is "least developed," see the world differently from Indo-Germans.

Key quotes (Kaufmann style):
"philosophizing is so far a kind of atavism of the highest order."
"the spell of certain grammatical functions is ultimately also the spell of PHYSIOLOGICAL valuations and racial conditions."
"philosophers within the domain of the Ural-Altaic languages ... look otherwise 'into the world'."

Philosophical implications: Even the most "critical or systematic" minds are bound by unconscious linguistic and bodily schemas. The supposed freedom of the thinker is itself a product of grammar and physiology. This directly prepares the free spirit's task (Part Two) of recognizing these constraints in order to experiment beyond them, and it deepens the critique of the atomistic 'I' and causa sui from nearby sections.

Why it matters: In an age of global philosophy and AI language models, Nietzsche reminds us that our deepest categories (subject, object, cause, being) may be provincial artifacts of Indo-European grammar. The free spirit cultivates suspicion toward the very structure of his own sentences.
Key Context: Early in Part One's psychological turn. Connects language, body, and the "common-household of the soul" to the will to power and perspectivism that follow.
HIGHLIGHTED QUOTES
