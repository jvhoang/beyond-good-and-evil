FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 20

Identifier: 20
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Philosophical ideas are never optional or autonomously evolving inventions. They grow in intimate connection and relationship, like the collective members of the fauna of a continent. Under an invisible spell, the most diverse philosophers unfailingly fill in again a definite fundamental scheme of possible philosophies. However independent they may feel, something within them leads them in definite order: the innate methodology and relationship of their ideas. Their thinking is far less a discovery than a re-recognizing, a remembering, a return and home-coming to a far-off, ancient common-household of the soul out of which those ideas formerly grew. Philosophizing is a kind of atavism of the highest order.

Breakdown of the argument:
• The wonderful family resemblance of all Indian, Greek, and German philosophizing is easily explained by affinity of language. Owing to the common philosophy of grammar—the unconscious domination and guidance of similar grammatical functions—everything is prepared at the outset for a similar development and succession of philosophical systems.
• The spell of certain grammatical functions is ultimately also the spell of physiological valuations and racial conditions. Philosophers within the domain of the Ural-Altaic languages (where the conception of the subject is least developed) look otherwise “into the world” and will be found on paths of thought different from those of the Indo-Germans and Mussulmans.
• This exposes the myth of the sovereign, value-neutral thinker. All philosophy remains entangled in the body, language, and inherited drives.

Key Takeaway: Systems of thought are organic expressions of a drive (later named will to power). The philosopher’s “I” is a multiplicity, not a simple unity. There is no escape from perspective into pure objectivity.

Connections to the broader BGE project: This section deepens the Preface’s suspicion of dogmatism and seeds Part One’s critiques of the “I” (Section 17), atomism, causa sui (21), and nature’s conformity to law (22). It anticipates the free spirit who can consciously affirm and master these necessities rather than deny them.

Reading Tip (Kaufmann translation): Read slowly and notice how Nietzsche moves from the organic metaphor (fauna of a continent) to grammar to physiology to race. Ask yourself: Which grammatical habits in your own language shape what seems “obvious” or “necessary” in your thinking?

Why it matters today: In an age of large language models trained on massive corpora, the “spell of grammar” and inherited data is literal. Cognitive science confirms embodied and linguistic relativity (echoing Sapir-Whorf). Cultural and political worldviews often feel like inevitable truths precisely because they are expressions of deep, unexamined physiological and historical valuations. Nietzsche invites us to become aware of the ancient household we still inhabit.
Companion Polish: Treat this as a diagnostic tool for your own thinking. The section does not attack philosophy; it reveals its conditions so that stronger, more honest philosophers of the future can emerge. Use the slides to prompt reflection before or after reading the Kaufmann text.
