FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 13

Identifier: 13 • Will to Power, Not Self-Preservation • Part One
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
In one of the shortest but most consequential aphorisms of Part One, Nietzsche delivers a direct statement of a principle that will become central to the entire book and his later thought: life itself is will to power.

"Psychologists should bethink themselves before putting down the instinct of self-preservation as the cardinal instinct of an organic being. A living thing seeks above all to DISCHARGE its strength—life itself is WILL TO POWER; self-preservation is only one of the indirect and most frequent RESULTS thereof."

The warning against "SUPERFLUOUS teleological principles" is methodological as much as substantive. Self-preservation as the fundamental drive (an idea Nietzsche traces in part to Spinoza's inconsistency) adds an unnecessary layer. "It is thus, in effect, that method ordains, which must be essentially economy of principles."

This small section performs several crucial tasks in BGE. First, it decisively shifts the basic ontology of life away from reactive preservation or survival toward active, expansive discharge and overcoming. Self-preservation is demoted to a frequent but secondary consequence of a more fundamental drive: the will to express, expand, and discharge power. Second, it reinforces the book's polemic against teleology—reading purposes or final causes into nature where there are only forces and their outcomes. Third, by identifying life with will to power, it provides the positive counterpart to the negative critiques of atomism, faculties, and dualisms that dominate the early sections. The self is not a simple atom to be preserved; it is a dynamic constellation of drives whose fundamental tendency is toward strength and form-giving activity.

Key quotes (Kaufmann translation):
"Psychologists should bethink themselves before putting down the instinct of self-preservation as the cardinal instinct of an organic being."
"A living thing seeks above all to DISCHARGE its strength—life itself is WILL TO POWER; self-preservation is only one of the indirect and most frequent RESULTS thereof."
"In short, here, as everywhere else, let us beware of SUPERFLUOUS teleological principles!—one of which is the instinct of self-preservation (we owe it to Spinoza's inconsistency). It is thus, in effect, that method ordains, which must be essentially economy of principles."

Philosophical implications for truth, morality, power, and the free spirit: This is the first explicit naming of "will to power" in the book. It reorients psychology, biology, and ethics: the primary phenomenon is not the struggle to stay alive but the discharge of strength, the imposition of form, the expansion of power. Traditional moralities that prize self-denial, humility, or mere survival are exposed as misunderstandings or suppressions of life's fundamental character. For the free spirit and the philosopher of the future, this principle grounds the experimental, commanding, value-creating mode of existence. Truth itself becomes a function of power-interests and interpretive strength rather than disinterested preservation of "facts." The section advances the revaluation by replacing survivalist or preservationist accounts of life (common in both Darwinian and Christian thought) with an affirmative, artistic, power-affirming one.

Modern relevance is enormous. Evolutionary biology and popular culture still often misread Darwin through a narrow "struggle for existence" or "selfish gene" lens that overemphasizes preservation. Psychology, leadership theory, and self-help frequently prioritize "resilience," "safety," or "survival" over growth, expression, and risk. Nietzsche's formulation illuminates ambition, creativity, sport, art, entrepreneurship, and even apparently self-destructive behaviors as manifestations of strength-discharge rather than mere pathology or error. In an age obsessed with wellness, longevity, and risk-aversion, the insistence that a living thing seeks above all to discharge its strength offers a bracing counter-ideal: life as active overflowing rather than cautious maintenance. It remains a key resource for understanding power dynamics in politics, organizations, and personal development beyond simplistic survival narratives.
Key Context: Part One of BGE. First explicit formulation of "will to power" in the book; methodological call for economy of principles; shifts from critique of prejudices to positive ontology of life. Central to all later parts on morality, nobility, and the future philosopher.
