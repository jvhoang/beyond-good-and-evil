FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 1

Identifier: 1
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 1 turns philosophy’s most sacred assumption into an urgent problem. The “Will to Truth” has driven philosophers into hazardous territory and left behind perplexing questions. Nietzsche refuses to treat this drive as an unquestioned good. Instead, he poses the questions earlier thinkers largely avoided: Granted that we want the truth—WHY NOT RATHER untruth? And uncertainty? Even ignorance? The problem of the value of truth stops us in our tracks. It is a “rendezvous of questions.” Nietzsche dramatizes the danger with the image of Oedipus facing the Sphinx: “Which of us is the Oedipus here? Which the Sphinx?” Raising the question carries real risk—“For there is risk in raising it, perhaps there is no greater risk.” The free spirit must have the strength to ask it anyway.

Key Insight: Genuine philosophy does not begin with answers. It begins with the courage to interrogate its own most cherished value—the will to truth itself. The Sphinx offers no solutions; she compels the questioner to confront the risk and necessity of the question.

Connection to the book: The Preface’s “free, VERY free spirits” now face their first real test. This section’s suspicion of truth directly fuels the dismantling of value antitheses in Section 2, the naturalization of consciousness as instinctive in Section 3, and the affirmation that untruth can be a condition of life in Section 4. It launches the book’s central project: a revaluation that begins by turning the critical gaze upon the drive that has long defined Western philosophy.

Why it matters: In an age of “post-truth” rhetoric, scientific denial, and comforting narratives sold as facts, Nietzsche does not simply defend truth. He asks whether our attachment to it is always life-serving—or whether certain untruths, errors, and illusions have proven more vital to human flourishing than we usually admit. For readers of the Kaufmann translation, the free spirit must possess the strength to pose the question and live with its consequences.
Companion Note: The courage to question the will to truth is the first act of the free spirit. Everything that follows in BGE tests whether we can sustain that courage without collapsing into cynicism or new dogmas. Pair this section with the Preface slides: together they frame the experimental stance of the whole book. Use the visuals to anchor discussion before diving into Kaufmann’s text.
