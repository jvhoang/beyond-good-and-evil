FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886 • PART THREE

Section 45

The Religious Character and the Saint
Identifier: 45
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY (Faithful to Text)
In the broader context of Nietzsche's thought, Section 45 opens Part Three of Beyond Good and Evil ('What Is Religious' or 'The Religious Nature') by shifting from the epistemological and psychological critiques of Part One and the free spirit explorations of Part Two to a sustained psychological investigation of religion as a phenomenon of the human soul. Following his earlier works like Human, All Too Human and Daybreak, where religion is diagnosed as error and consolation, here Nietzsche treats the religious domain as the supreme 'hunting ground' for the born psychologist—the 'lover of the big hunt.' This aligns with BGE's overall project as a 'prelude to a philosophy of the future,' where old idols (including religious ones) are dismantled to clear space for new philosophers who will revalue values through will to power and perspectivism. The section positions Nietzsche himself as this solitary hunter, emphasizing that the history of the soul in homines religiosi requires immense personal experience (like Pascal's) combined with 'clear, wicked spirituality' to oversee it.

The main points and arguments: The preordained domain for the psychologist is the entire history of the soul—its limits, inner experiences, heights, depths, and unexhausted possibilities. The solitary individual confronts a 'virgin forest' and desires hundreds of assistants and trained hounds, but scholars fail precisely when the 'BIG hunt' and danger begin, losing keenness. To understand the history of knowledge and conscience in religious souls demands personal bruising experience plus overarching vision. Ultimately, one must do everything oneself; curiosity is the most agreeable vice, rewarded even on earth.

Key Kaufmann quotes: 'The human soul and its limits, the range of man's inner experiences hitherto attained... this is the preordained hunting-domain for a born psychologist and lover of a "big hunt".' 'But how often must he say despairingly to himself: "A single individual! alas, only a single individual! and this great forest, this virgin forest!"' The saint and religious neurosis are later extensions of this hunt (Sec 47).
