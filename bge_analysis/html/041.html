FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 41

Identifier: 41 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 41 offers the practical, dangerous test of the free spirit: "One has to test oneself to see that one is destined for independence and command—and do it at the right time." One must not dodge these tests, though they are the most dangerous game and can ultimately be judged only by oneself.

Do not remain stuck to a person—not even the most loved (every person is a prison, also a nook). Not to a fatherland, not even one that suffers and needs help most. Not to pity, even for higher men whose rare torture we glimpse. Not to a science, even if it lures with precious finds saved for us. Not to one's own detachment—the danger of the flier who flees ever higher. Not even to one's own virtues, such as hospitality, which can turn into a vice for superior souls.

Key quote (Kaufmann): "One must know how to conserve oneself: the hardest test of independence."

Modern companion insight: In an age of tribal loyalties, identity politics, virtue signaling, endless "causes," and the moral prestige of attachment and activism, Nietzsche's call to test and conserve the self against every form of sticking—including our own cherished self-image as good, caring, or committed—is a bracing antidote to ideological capture, burnout, and loss of autonomy.
Key Context: Practical tests of free-spirited independence and self-conservation; active command over attachments.
