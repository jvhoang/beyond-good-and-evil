FRIEDRICH NIETZSCHE • 1886

Section 37

Identifier: 37 • Part Two: The Free Spirit
EXTENSIVE SUMMARY
Section 37 is a witty, sharp rebuke: "'What? Doesn't this mean, to speak with the vulgar: God is refuted, but the devil is not?' On the contrary! On the contrary, my friends! And the devil – who forces you to speak with the vulgar?"

Nietzsche refuses to let the will-to-power hypothesis be flattened into a simple moral inversion or Manichaean drama. Those who hear "beyond good and evil" as merely "evil is now good" are speaking in the vulgar tongue and dragging the idea back into the moralistic frame it was meant to escape. The free spirit does not play that game.

Modern companion insight: The same vulgarization happens constantly today—new ideologies are immediately recast as "the new good vs the old evil" or hero/villain narratives. Nietzsche reminds the free spirit to keep the hypothesis experimental and anti-moralistic, refusing to let it become another dogmatic religion of inversion.
Key Context: Defends the will-to-power hypothesis against moralistic or vulgar reduction (continuing Sec 36).
